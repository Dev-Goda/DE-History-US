CHAPTER: Ch. 19 – American Empire (Deep)

Q: The Guano Islands Act (1856) was an early example of U.S. expansion because:
1: It established the first U.S. military bases in the Pacific
2: It allowed Americans to claim uninhabited islands containing guano for use as fertilizer
3: It gave the U.S. exclusive trading rights with Pacific island nations
4: It authorized the Navy to patrol shipping lanes around U.S. island territories
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Before synthetic fertilizers, guano was one of the world's most valuable agricultural inputs. The Act let U.S. citizens claim any guano-rich island, resulting in over 100 claims — economic-driven proto-imperialism that set the logic for later territorial expansion.

Q: In Hawaiian culture, "haole" refers to:
1: Native Hawaiian royalty
2: Foreigners, particularly white individuals
3: Agricultural laborers brought from Asia
4: Native Hawaiian religious leaders
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: "Haole" literally means "foreign" in Hawaiian. Over time it became associated specifically with white Americans and Europeans who came for trade and agriculture. The "Big Five" haole sugar companies eventually dominated the islands' economy and politics.

Q: The "Big Five" in Hawaii were:
1: Five Native Hawaiian kingdoms united under Queen Liliuokalani
2: Five major sugar companies that dominated Hawaii's economy and influenced politics
3: Five military forts the U.S. built before annexation
4: Five reform groups opposing U.S. annexation
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Big Five (Castle & Cooke, Alexander & Baldwin, C. Brewer, Theo. Davies, and American Factors) controlled sugar production, banking, shipping, and retail. Their economic power translated into political pressure for annexation to avoid U.S. sugar tariffs.

Q: The U.S. sought which key strategic asset in Hawaii?
1: Control of Hawaii's gold and silver reserves
2: A naval base at Pearl Harbor
3: A fueling station for merchant ships crossing to Japan
4: An army training base in a tropical environment
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Pearl Harbor's deep, protected harbor made it ideal for a naval base — a coaling and repair station in the middle of the Pacific enabling the U.S. Navy to project power across Asia. Mahan's theories made such forward bases seem essential to national greatness.

Q: Queen Liliuokalani was significant because:
1: She invited the U.S. to annex Hawaii
2: She was the last Hawaiian monarch, overthrown by American planters in 1893
3: She signed the treaty formally ceding Hawaii to the U.S.
4: She successfully expelled American business interests in the 1880s
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Liliuokalani tried to restore royal power and limit American planters' political influence. In 1893, a small group of businessmen backed by U.S. Marines overthrew her government. President Cleveland called it illegal, but Congress annexed Hawaii anyway in 1898.

Q: Mahan's central argument in "The Influence of Sea Power" (1890) was:
1: Land armies were more important than navies
2: The U.S. should remain isolationist
3: National greatness required a strong navy, overseas bases, and control of trade routes
4: The U.S. should focus on trade with Latin America
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Mahan studied how Britain became dominant and concluded sea power was the key — specifically a battle fleet supported by overseas naval bases and coaling stations controlling world trade routes. His ideas directly shaped U.S. naval and foreign policy for decades.

Q: Mahan's theories most directly influenced which policy decision?
1: Construction of the transcontinental railroad
2: Expansion of the U.S. Navy and pursuit of overseas territories
3: Passage of the Chinese Exclusion Act
4: Creation of the Homestead Act
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: After publication, Congress dramatically expanded the Navy — building modern steel battleships. Roosevelt, McKinley, and other leaders explicitly cited Mahan in justifying overseas bases in Hawaii, the Philippines, and the Caribbean.

Q: "Yellow journalism" was significant to the Spanish-American War because:
1: It accurately reported Spanish atrocities providing Congress with evidence
2: It used sensationalized and exaggerated reporting to build public support for war
3: It exposed U.S. government corruption leading to conflict
4: It opposed the war making Congressional approval harder
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hearst's New York Journal and Pulitzer's New York World competed for readers with sensational, often fabricated stories of Spanish brutality in Cuba. Hearst allegedly said "You furnish the pictures, I'll furnish the war" — illustrating how media manufactured the public pressure that helped push the U.S. to war.

Q: The U.S.S. Maine was relevant to the Spanish-American War because:
1: It was the flagship Dewey used at Manila Bay
2: Its explosion in Havana Harbor was used by yellow journalists to inflame opinion against Spain
3: It was captured by Spain and used to blockade Cuba
4: It carried troops who fought in the first land battle
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Maine exploded in Havana Harbor in February 1898, killing 266 American sailors. The cause remains disputed — probably an accidental internal explosion — but yellow journalists immediately blamed Spain with "Remember the Maine!" providing the public pressure to declare war.

Q: Commodore Dewey's victory at Manila Bay was strategically important because:
1: It ended Spanish resistance in Cuba
2: It secured U.S. control over the Philippines by destroying the Spanish Pacific fleet
3: It allowed the U.S. to blockade Spain's coast
4: It demonstrated the Army would win the war
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: On May 1, 1898, Dewey's squadron destroyed the Spanish fleet without losing a single American life. This gave the U.S. a Pacific foothold it hadn't planned for — suddenly the Philippines were there for the taking, triggering the much larger question of American imperialism.

Q: The Treaty of Paris (1898) resulted in the U.S. acquiring:
1: Cuba, Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands
2: Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines, with Cuba becoming a U.S. protectorate
3: The Philippines, Guam, and Cuba as official U.S. territories
4: Puerto Rico and the Philippines only, with Spain retaining Guam
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Spain ceded Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines (for $20 million) to the U.S. Cuba became nominally independent but was essentially a U.S. protectorate under the Platt Amendment, which gave the U.S. the right to intervene and secured the naval base at Guantanamo Bay.

Q: Emilio Aguinaldo's role in the Philippine-American War was:
1: He allied with the U.S. against Filipino nationalists
2: He was a Filipino nationalist who first fought Spain then led resistance against U.S. annexation
3: He negotiated the peaceful transfer of the Philippines to the U.S.
4: He was a U.S.-appointed governor who suppressed the independence movement
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Aguinaldo had allied with U.S. forces believing independence would follow. When the U.S. kept the Philippines, he led the Philippine Republic's army against American forces in a brutal guerrilla war — the U.S. winning through military force and eventual political concession.

Q: The Philippine-American War was controversial because:
1: It showed the U.S. could not defeat a guerrilla insurgency
2: It contradicted U.S. claims of supporting self-determination while engaging in brutal colonial rule
3: It caused the U.S. to immediately grant the Philippines independence
4: It was fought without Congressional approval
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The U.S. had justified war with Spain partly by claiming Spain violated Cubans' right to self-determination. Denying Filipino independence exposed this as hypocritical — the U.S. was doing what it had condemned Spain for doing. American soldiers' use of torture further inflamed critics.

Q: The American Anti-Imperialist League argued:
1: The U.S. economy needed all resources at home
2: Imperialism violated American democratic principles and contradicted the nation's founding ideals
3: Expansion would lead to war with Britain and Germany
4: Colonized peoples would eventually invade the U.S.
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The League (including Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie, ex-President Cleveland) argued that the Declaration's principle of government by consent of the governed was incompatible with colonial rule over peoples who hadn't consented — making imperialism un-American.

Q: Notable members of the American Anti-Imperialist League included:
1: Theodore Roosevelt and Alfred Thayer Mahan
2: Mark Twain and Andrew Carnegie
3: William Jennings Bryan and Eugene Debs
4: Commodore Dewey and Secretary of State John Hay
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: It's striking that Carnegie — who made his fortune from industrial exploitation — opposed imperialism on principle. Twain wrote bitter satirical attacks on U.S. colonial policy. Their coalition was politically strange but united by the belief that imperialism betrayed American founding ideals.

Q: John Hay's Open Door Policy was designed to:
1: Allow unrestricted immigration from Asia
2: Ensure equal trading rights for all major powers in China, protecting U.S. commercial interests
3: Open the Panama Canal to all nations without tariffs
4: Give U.S. companies exclusive access to Chinese markets
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: As European powers carved China into exclusive "spheres of influence," the U.S. (arriving late) pushed for equal access for all nations. This protected U.S. commercial interests without the expense of colonization, establishing a model of economic imperialism without formal territorial empire.

Q: The Boxer Rebellion resulted in the U.S.:
1: Remaining neutral to protect the Open Door Policy
2: Siding with Chinese nationalists against European powers
3: Joining an international military force to suppress the rebellion
4: Annexing Chinese coastal ports
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The Boxers laid siege to the foreign legation quarter in Beijing. The U.S. joined the eight-nation multinational relief force that ended the siege — further cementing U.S. entanglement in Asian affairs and reinforcing the Open Door framework.

Q: Roosevelt's "Great White Fleet" (1907-1909) was primarily meant to:
1: Escort merchant ships through dangerous Pacific waters
2: Demonstrate American naval power and assert global presence
3: Evacuate American citizens from war zones in Asia
4: Supply U.S. Pacific territories with military equipment
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Roosevelt sent 16 gleaming white battleships on a world tour partly to impress Japan (with whom tensions were high) and partly to show Americans and Congress that the U.S. was now a genuine world naval power requiring continued investment.

Q: The Roosevelt Corollary (1904) expanded the Monroe Doctrine by:
1: Extending U.S. protection to European colonies in the hemisphere
2: Asserting the U.S. right to intervene in Latin American nations to maintain stability
3: Promising the U.S. would not interfere in European conflicts
4: Creating a military alliance with all Latin American nations
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The original Monroe Doctrine (1823) told Europe to stay out of the Americas. Roosevelt's Corollary added that the U.S. would step in to manage Latin American affairs if nations couldn't "maintain order" — essentially claiming U.S. police power over the entire Western Hemisphere.

Q: Wilson's invasion of Veracruz was triggered by:
1: Mexico's refusal to repay American loans
2: Mexican forces crossing into Texas
3: The arrest of U.S. sailors by Mexican authorities
4: Mexico's secret alliance with Germany
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Mexican authorities in Tampico briefly detained U.S. sailors loading supplies (1914). The local commander quickly apologized, but Wilson demanded a formal 21-gun salute to the American flag. When Mexico refused, Wilson ordered occupation of Veracruz — widely seen as a disproportionate overreaction.

Q: The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) was historically significant because:
1: It was the first U.S. law to ban immigration based on race/nationality
2: It temporarily suspended Chinese immigration for 10 years before repeal
3: It was passed to protect Chinese workers from employer discrimination
4: It only applied to California
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: The Chinese Exclusion Act was a shameful first — the only U.S. law to explicitly ban immigration by a specific nationality. It was not "temporary" in practice; it was renewed repeatedly and made permanent in 1902. It wasn't fully repealed until 1943, when China was a U.S. WWII ally.

Q: The Immigration Act of 1924 established quotas that:
1: Welcomed all nations' immigrants equally if they had skills
2: Favored Northern Europeans while severely limiting Southern/Eastern Europeans and Asians
3: Banned all immigration for ten years
4: Only restricted nations without U.S. trade agreements
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The 1924 Act's national-origin quotas were designed to preserve Anglo-Saxon "character" — capping immigration from Italy, Poland, and Russia at tiny numbers while allowing generous British and German quotas. It also effectively banned Asian immigration entirely, staying in force until 1965.

Q: U.S. interests in the Middle East in the 1800s focused on:
1: Oil reserves, military bases, and the Suez Canal
2: Religion, education, and trade routes — NOT oil
3: Establishing colonies to compete with Britain and France
4: Converting Muslim populations to Christianity by force
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Before the oil age, U.S. engagement in the Middle East was driven by Protestant missionaries (founding colleges like AUB and AUC), merchants seeking routes to Asia, and diplomats managing the declining Ottoman Empire. Oil-driven Middle East policy only emerged after WWI.

Q: The Spanish-American War is sometimes called a "splendid little war" because:
1: The U.S. suffered few casualties while winning enormous territorial gains quickly
2: It was fought entirely at sea with no land battles
3: It united North and South in their first shared military cause since the Civil War
4: It was fought entirely by volunteers with no draft
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Secretary of State John Hay coined "splendid little war" — the U.S. fought for about four months, suffered relatively few combat deaths, and emerged with Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines. The far longer and bloodier Philippine-American War that followed was considerably less "splendid."
