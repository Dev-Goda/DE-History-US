CHAPTER: 22 — The 1920s | Republican Politics

Q: Harding's 1920 campaign slogan "Return to Normalcy" promised:
1: Military rearmament to prevent future wars in Europe
2: A renewed push for civil rights after wartime racial tensions
3: Aggressive federal regulation of monopolies and trusts
4: Stability and a retreat from WWI disruption and Progressive Era activism
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Harding's slogan appealed to Americans exhausted by WWI and the Progressive Era's moral crusades. "Normalcy" meant pro-business, limited government, and a step back from Wilsonian idealism — not a reform agenda.

Q: The Teapot Dome Scandal involved:
1: Vote-rigging in the 1920 election uncovered by the Justice Department
2: Secret bribes paid to have federal oil reserves leased to private companies
3: Illegal sale of U.S. war bonds to foreign governments after WWI
4: Kickbacks to Harding's cabinet from railroad companies seeking land grants
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Interior Secretary Albert Fall secretly leased federal oil reserves at Teapot Dome, Wyoming to private oil companies in exchange for bribes. Fall became the first Cabinet member imprisoned for crimes committed in office. The scandal defined Harding's legacy.

Q: Calvin Coolidge's governing philosophy was best captured by the phrase:
1: "Speak softly and carry a big stick"
2: "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself"
3: "The chief business of the American people is business"
4: "Return to Normalcy"
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Coolidge's famous quote perfectly encapsulates his presidency: pro-business, low taxes, minimal regulation. He championed cutting the top income tax rate from 66% to 20%, believing a healthy business climate would lift all Americans.

Q: Herbert Hoover lost popularity so thoroughly during the Depression that the shantytowns built by the unemployed were sarcastically named:
1: Hoovervilles
2: President's Camps
3: Bonus Villages
4: Hoover Havens
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: "Hoovervilles" was a biting satirical name for the makeshift shantytowns that sprang up across American cities. The name reflected widespread public blame for Hoover's perceived failure to act decisively during the economic catastrophe.

Q: The 19th Amendment (1920) granted:
1: Women the right to vote
2: Prohibition of alcohol manufacture and sale
3: Direct election of U.S. Senators by popular vote
4: Equal civil rights protection regardless of race or gender
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: The 19th Amendment, the culmination of decades of suffragist activism, prohibited denying the vote based on sex. It took effect in 1920, in time for that year's presidential election.

Q: The 18th Amendment (1920) established:
1: Women's suffrage across all states
2: The graduated federal income tax
3: The direct election of U.S. Senators
4: Prohibition — banning the manufacture and sale of alcohol
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: The 18th Amendment was the product of the temperance movement, which argued alcohol destroyed families and productivity. Ironically, it fueled organized crime as bootlegging became enormously profitable.

Q: Alice Paul's major legislative goal after winning women's suffrage was:
1: A federal law mandating equal pay for equal work
2: A constitutional amendment guaranteeing women equal access to university education
3: A law prohibiting gender discrimination in federal hiring
4: The Equal Rights Amendment to guarantee full legal equality for women
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: After the 19th Amendment passed, Paul immediately pivoted to the ERA, arguing that suffrage alone was insufficient — women still faced systematic legal discrimination in employment, property rights, and family law. Congress introduced but ultimately defeated the ERA in this era.

Q: Herbert Hoover's 1928 opponent, Al Smith, was attacked on the campaign trail for:
1: His support for labor unions and suspected socialist sympathies
2: His connections to Tammany Hall and machine corruption
3: His lack of military service during WWI
4: Being Catholic and opposing Prohibition
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Al Smith, the first Catholic nominee of a major party, faced brutal anti-Catholic bigotry — many Protestant Americans feared a Catholic president would take orders from the Pope. His opposition to Prohibition also cost him votes in rural, Protestant America.

Q: The Flapper represented the 1920s "New Woman" primarily through:
1: Organizing politically to extend suffrage to all disenfranchised groups
2: Demanding equal pay and working conditions in factories
3: Defying Victorian gender norms through fashion, music, dancing, and social behavior
4: Entering professional careers previously closed to women, such as law and medicine
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The flapper — bobbed hair, short skirts, dancing to jazz — was a cultural statement, not primarily a political one. She rejected the constraints of 19th-century femininity (modesty, domesticity, chastity) in favor of pleasure, visibility, and self-expression.

Q: The Harlem Renaissance can best be described as:
1: A government program to provide arts funding to African American communities in New York
2: A real estate development that reshaped Harlem's demographics in the 1920s
3: An African American artistic, literary, and intellectual flowering centered in Harlem, NYC
4: A political movement to elect Black candidates to New York City office
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Writers like Langston Hughes and Zora Neale Hurston, musicians like Duke Ellington, and visual artists collectively created a new Black cultural identity that challenged racist stereotypes and asserted Black dignity and creativity on the national stage.

Q: The primary target of 1920s Fundamentalist Christianity was:
1: Darwinian evolution and the broader embrace of modern science
2: Catholic immigration, which Fundamentalists viewed as anti-American
3: Jazz music, which many Fundamentalists associated with moral decay
4: The suffrage movement and women entering public life
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Fundamentalists believed the Bible was literal truth. Darwin's theory of evolution, which contradicted a literal reading of Genesis, was their primary target. The Scopes Trial became the movement's defining moment and public battleground.

Q: The Scopes Trial (1925) involved a Tennessee teacher prosecuted for:
1: Distributing pacifist literature opposing U.S. entry into WWI
2: Allowing racially integrated seating in his classroom
3: Refusing to lead students in mandatory prayer
4: Teaching Darwinian evolution in violation of state law
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: John Scopes violated Tennessee's Butler Act, which prohibited teaching evolution in public schools. Clarence Darrow defended Scopes; William Jennings Bryan prosecuted. Though Scopes was convicted, the trial exposed Fundamentalism to national ridicule.

Q: Installment buying, which boomed in the 1920s, allowed consumers to:
1: Return goods to any participating store for store credit toward future purchases
2: Pool savings with neighbors to collectively buy expensive goods at wholesale prices
3: Receive government-subsidized loans to buy labor-saving home appliances
4: Purchase major goods on credit, paying in scheduled installments over time
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: By 1927, over 60% of cars were sold on credit. Installment buying extended to refrigerators, radios, and furniture — creating a consumer debt culture that looked prosperous but left households dangerously exposed when the Depression hit.

Q: The Jazz Age label reflects Jazz's cultural significance because:
1: Jazz musicians were the decade's highest-paid performers by a significant margin
2: Jazz's origins in Black New Orleans communities gave it a revolutionary cultural force that spread nationally via radio and records
3: Jazz was the first American music genre to achieve international popularity in Europe
4: Jazz performers were the most politically active group in the 1920s cultural landscape
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Jazz originated in Black New Orleans communities, blending African rhythms, blues, and ragtime. Its spread via radio and records carried it from Black nightclubs to white mainstream culture — a complex cultural exchange that reflected both racial appropriation and cross-racial influence.

Q: Henry Ford's assembly line transformed American life primarily because:
1: It created unionized manufacturing jobs that raised working-class wages significantly
2: It proved that American manufacturing could produce goods faster than European competitors
3: It made the Model-T affordable for ordinary Americans, democratizing car ownership
4: It pioneered safety standards that dramatically reduced workplace injuries
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: By producing a Model-T every 10 seconds by 1925, Ford slashed costs until a factory worker could afford the product he built. Car registrations leaped from 9 million in 1920 to 27 million by 1929, reshaping American geography, culture, and commerce.

CHAPTER: 22 — The 1920s | Popular Culture & Culture Wars

Q: The Jazz Singer (1927) was a landmark in film history because it:
1: Was the first film to feature an African American actor in a leading role
2: Introduced synchronized sound to motion pictures, ending the silent film era
3: Was the first American film to earn over $10 million at the box office
4: Was directed by the first woman to direct a Hollywood feature film
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Jazz Singer, starring Al Jolson, used the Vitaphone sound-on-disc system to synchronize dialogue and music with the film. It was the transition point between silent films — which had dominated since the 1890s — and the "talkie" era.

Q: By 1930, approximately what fraction of U.S. homes had a radio?
1: One in ten
2: One in four
3: Nearly one half
4: Three in four
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Radio's penetration was remarkable — from almost nothing in 1920 to nearly half of U.S. homes by 1930. NBC and CBS built national audiences, creating mass popular culture for the first time: shared news, music, sports, and the first "soap operas."

Q: The KKK revival of the 1920s differed from its Reconstruction-era predecessor because it:
1: Was primarily a Southern organization with no national political ambitions
2: Focused exclusively on opposing Black voting rights and political participation
3: Now targeted Catholics, Jews, and immigrants in addition to Black Americans, achieving membership in the millions
4: Operated primarily through legal lobbying rather than violence and intimidation
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The 1920s KKK was a national phenomenon reaching millions of members, including significant presence in Northern states like Indiana. It expanded its targets to fit nativist anxieties of the era — immigrants, Catholics, and Jews joined Black Americans as targets.

Q: Congress responded to 1920s nativist sentiment by:
1: Passing the Chinese Exclusion Act Extension banning all Asian immigration
2: Requiring all immigrants to pass English literacy and civic tests before admission
3: Creating a federal commission to evaluate and deport suspected radical immigrants
4: Enacting strict immigration quota laws targeting Southern and Eastern Europeans
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: The Emergency Quota Act (1921) and Immigration Act (1924) established national-origins quotas that deliberately privileged Northern and Western European immigrants while sharply limiting Italians, Poles, Greeks, and other Southern/Eastern Europeans. Asian immigration was effectively banned entirely.

Q: Babe Ruth's cultural significance in the 1920s extended beyond baseball because:
1: He was the first professional athlete to publicly support civil rights for Black Americans
2: He became such a towering public figure that he was more famous than most politicians of the era
3: He pioneered the use of endorsement deals that transformed how athletes earned income
4: He was the first athlete to have a nationally broadcast radio contract
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Ruth's superhuman home run records (including 60 in a single season in 1927) made him a larger-than-life figure who embodied the decade's excess and optimism. Politicians sought his endorsement; children idolized him; he was a genuinely national icon in a way few athletes had been before.

Q: Charles Lindbergh's significance in the 1920s was his:
1: Setting the land speed record in a gasoline-powered automobile across the American West
2: First solo nonstop transatlantic flight from New York to Paris in 1927
3: First commercial passenger airline service connecting New York and Chicago
4: Designing the engine that made Ford's Model-T so efficient and affordable
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Lindbergh's 33.5-hour solo flight made him the decade's greatest hero. The "Spirit of St. Louis" flight electrified the world and symbolized the era's faith in technology, individualism, and American can-do spirit.

Q: Mary Pickford's cultural significance in the 1920s was that she:
1: Founded the first Hollywood film studio owned and operated entirely by a woman
2: Was the first woman to win an Academy Award for Best Director
3: Popularized the flapper image and new gender norms through her film star persona
4: Advocated publicly for the Equal Rights Amendment using her celebrity platform
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: "America's Sweetheart" was simultaneously a traditional girl-next-door and a glamorous modern woman. Her films and public image helped normalize the flapper aesthetic for millions of women who might not otherwise have encountered it directly.

Q: Consumer spending on appliances grew by over 120% between 1919 and 1929 because:
1: Federal subsidies made refrigerators and washing machines affordable for working families
2: Labor unions negotiated contracts that gave workers enough income to afford luxury goods
3: New technology, advertising, and easy installment credit created a mass consumer economy
4: Falling immigration reduced competition for manufacturing jobs, driving up worker wages
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The three-part engine of 1920s consumerism: new products (refrigerators, radios, vacuum cleaners), national advertising that created desire for them, and installment credit that let people buy them before they'd saved the money. This was the birth of modern consumer culture.

CHAPTER: 22 — The 1920s | People Summary

Q: Which president's administration is most associated with pro-business tax cuts, reducing the top income tax rate from 66% to 20%?
1: Calvin Coolidge
2: Herbert Hoover
3: Warren G. Harding
4: Franklin D. Roosevelt
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Coolidge and his Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon aggressively cut taxes on the wealthy, arguing that money freed at the top would flow through the economy. Critics noted it dramatically widened inequality; defenders credited it with the 1920s boom.

Q: Which 1920s figure is most associated with making the automobile affordable for average Americans through mass production?
1: Andrew Carnegie
2: J.P. Morgan
3: John D. Rockefeller
4: Henry Ford
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Ford's Highland Park plant pioneered the moving assembly line. By breaking car production into simple, repetitive steps, Ford cut the time and cost of production so dramatically that by the mid-1920s, a Ford factory worker could buy a Model-T with a few months' wages.

Q: Which 1920s sports figure became so famous he was said to be more well-known than the president?
1: Jack Dempsey
2: Red Grange
3: Charles Lindbergh
4: Babe Ruth
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Ruth's fame was extraordinary even by modern standards. His home run records, his outsized personality, his charitable visits to sick children — all made him a truly national figure. His 1927 salary of $80,000 was higher than President Coolidge's.

CHAPTER: 23 — The Great Depression

Q: Black Tuesday refers to:
1: The day President Hoover declared a national banking emergency in 1930
2: The day the Smoot-Hawley Tariff was signed, triggering global retaliation
3: October 29, 1929, when the stock market suffered its catastrophic crash
4: The day the Bonus Army was forcibly dispersed from Washington D.C.
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: On Black Tuesday, stock values collapsed in panic selling. U.S. Steel fell from $262 to $22; GM from $73 to $8. However, only 2.5% of Americans owned stocks — the crash revealed deeper structural weaknesses that would cause the Depression, not cause it alone.

Q: The Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930) worsened the Great Depression by:
1: Raising prices on domestic goods, making them unaffordable for unemployed Americans
2: Redirecting federal revenue from relief programs to pay down the national debt
3: Triggering retaliatory tariffs from other nations that collapsed global trade from $36 billion to $12 billion
4: Causing European nations to default on WWI loans, destabilizing American banks
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: When the U.S. raised tariffs to record levels, trading partners retaliated. Global trade collapsed by two-thirds. U.S. exports fell 78%. This destroyed American agricultural and manufacturing exports, deepening unemployment that was already rising from domestic causes.

Q: Hoover's "associationalism" was a philosophy that held:
1: Federal, state, and local governments should share equal responsibility for economic relief
2: Voluntary cooperation between businesses and private charities — not direct government action — should address economic hardship
3: Labor unions and corporations should negotiate wages and prices without government interference
4: The president should convene associations of business leaders to coordinate economic recovery plans
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hoover genuinely believed that direct federal relief would undermine American self-reliance and create dangerous dependency. His POUR (President's Organization for Unemployment Relief) relied on private charity — which was quickly overwhelmed by a Depression that affected one-quarter of all workers.

Q: What was the Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC), and why was it controversial?
1: A federal agency that bought failing farms, called controversial because it excluded Southern farmers
2: A government bank that provided loans to states for infrastructure, called controversial for bypassing Congress
3: A wartime emergency agency Hoover reactivated, called controversial because it was unconstitutional under peacetime law
4: A Hoover program that lent government money to banks and corporations while providing no direct help to ordinary unemployed Americans
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Critics like Fiorello LaGuardia called the RFC a "millionaire's dole" — the government was bailing out financial institutions at the top while refusing direct aid to the unemployed at the bottom. It perfectly encapsulated Hoover's ideological blind spot.

Q: Hoovervilles were significant because they:
1: Provided government-organized emergency shelter for the worst-affected unemployed
2: Symbolized the human cost of the Depression and the public's verdict on Hoover's failed response
3: Were only found in industrial cities, revealing the Depression's urban concentration
4: Demonstrated that churches and charities were capable of handling Depression-era poverty
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: These shantytowns of scrap wood and corrugated metal appeared in parks and on vacant lots in every major city. The bitterly satirical name said everything about where Americans placed the blame for the disaster.

Q: The Dust Bowl was caused by:
1: A combination of severe drought and decades of agricultural mismanagement that stripped the plains of protective topsoil
2: Federal irrigation projects that accidentally depleted the Great Plains water table
3: Industrial pollution from oil refineries that destroyed fertile soil in Oklahoma and Texas
4: Overuse of new chemical fertilizers that made soil brittle and susceptible to erosion
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Years of deep plowing had destroyed the native grasses that held Great Plains topsoil in place. When drought struck (1932–1936), there was nothing to anchor the soil. Massive dust storms — "Black Blizzards" — buried farms and drove hundreds of thousands from their homes.

Q: The Bonus Army of 1932 consisted of:
1: Active-duty military troops Hoover deployed to enforce emergency banking regulations
2: WWI veterans who marched to Washington demanding early payment of bonuses promised for 1945
3: Unemployed factory workers who marched on Washington to demand a federal jobs program
4: Farmers from the Dust Bowl region who marched to demand agricultural price supports
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: About 20,000 veterans camped in Washington, living in shantytowns near the Capitol. When Congress refused their demands and Hoover ordered MacArthur to clear them, MacArthur exceeded his orders — using cavalry, bayonets, and tear gas against veterans on American soil. It was a political catastrophe for Hoover.

Q: The First New Deal (1933–1934) focused primarily on:
1: Long-term structural reforms like Social Security and union rights
2: Immediate relief and economic recovery through emergency programs
3: Reforming the Supreme Court to remove obstacles to federal regulation
4: Military preparedness as an economic stimulus and job-creation strategy
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The First New Deal was a rapid response to emergency: bank deposit insurance (FDIC), emergency employment (CCC, PWA), agricultural price supports (AAA), and industrial coordination (NRA). Relief and recovery dominated; structural reform came in the Second New Deal.

Q: The Social Security Act (1935) created:
1: Universal healthcare coverage for all Americans
2: A federal jobs guarantee ensuring employment for all willing workers
3: Retirement pensions for elderly workers and unemployment insurance for the jobless
4: A minimum wage and maximum hours standard for all American workers
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Social Security established two pillars: old-age insurance (funded by payroll taxes on workers and employers) and unemployment insurance. It was the most transformative piece of New Deal legislation — creating the permanent safety net that still exists today.

Q: The Wagner Act (1935) was a landmark labor law because it:
1: Established a federal minimum wage of $0.25 per hour for all industries
2: Guaranteed workers' right to organize unions and bargain collectively, backed by federal enforcement
3: Banned child labor in all industries engaged in interstate commerce
4: Required corporations with over 100 employees to share profits with workers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Wagner Act (also called the National Labor Relations Act) created the NLRB to enforce workers' right to organize without employer interference. Union membership surged in the years after its passage — from about 3 million to over 10 million by 1941.

Q: The CCC, WPA, and PWA all shared which common purpose?
1: They provided emergency loans to banks to prevent further failures
2: They provided direct cash relief payments to unemployed Americans
3: They paid farmers to reduce crop production to raise agricultural prices
4: They used government spending to create jobs and build public infrastructure
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: The CCC employed young men in conservation projects; the PWA funded large-scale construction (dams, bridges, schools); the WPA employed millions in both construction and arts (murals, theater, writing). Together they demonstrated that the federal government could be an employer of last resort.

Q: A bank run occurs when:
1: Customers simultaneously withdraw deposits in panic, causing even a solvent bank to run out of cash and fail
2: Bank executives attempt to transfer funds to offshore accounts before regulators arrive
3: Federal regulators move to seize control of a bank suspected of fraud
4: A bank makes risky investments that cause it to lose its depositors' money
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Bank runs are self-fulfilling panics: if everyone believes a bank is failing, everyone withdraws at once, and the bank fails even if it was previously sound. The FDIC (created in 1933) ended bank runs by guaranteeing deposits — if your money is safe regardless, there's no reason to panic-withdraw.

Q: FDR's court-packing plan (1937) failed because:
1: The Supreme Court declared the plan unconstitutional before Congress could vote
2: FDR withdrew it after several New Deal opponents on the Court retired voluntarily
3: Congress blocked it, and its failure marked the effective end of major New Deal legislation
4: Three conservative justices changed their votes on New Deal cases, removing FDR's justification
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Even many New Deal supporters in Congress opposed the plan as executive overreach. Its defeat was a major political defeat for FDR and effectively ended the era of landmark New Deal legislation, even though FDR was personally popular enough to win reelection in 1940.

Q: Eleanor Roosevelt's historical significance goes beyond being First Lady because:
1: She ran for a New York Senate seat while FDR was president, winning in 1936
2: She served as the U.S. Ambassador to the United Nations after WWII
3: She was appointed to FDR's cabinet as Secretary of Labor, the first woman in a cabinet post
4: She was a visible and influential advocate for the poor, women, and African Americans in a way no First Lady had been before
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Eleanor held press conferences (for women reporters only, forcing outlets to hire female journalists), traveled the country visiting relief programs, advocated for Black Americans when FDR was politically unwilling to, and represented a new, activist model of the First Lady role.

Q: Huey Long challenged FDR from which political direction, and with what proposal?
1: From the left, with his "Share Our Wealth" plan calling for massive redistribution through taxation
2: From the center, arguing FDR needed to move faster on banking reform
3: From the right, arguing the New Deal was socialism that would destroy American freedom
4: From the far left, calling for the outright nationalization of major corporations
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Long's "Share Our Wealth" proposed capping personal fortunes at $5 million and guaranteeing every family a minimum income of $2,500. It was enormously popular — he claimed 7 million members in his organization — and posed a genuine political threat to FDR until Long's assassination in 1935.

Q: Dorothea Lange's "Migrant Mother" photograph became historically significant because:
1: It was the first photograph published on the front page of a major national newspaper
2: It won the first Pulitzer Prize ever awarded for a photograph
3: It put a human face on Dust Bowl suffering and became the iconic image of the Depression
4: It led directly to Congress passing emergency relief legislation for California farmworkers
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Lange's photograph of Florence Owens Thompson — hollow-cheeked, nursing children, surrounded by poverty — became the Depression's defining image. It demonstrated the power of documentary photography to create political empathy and move public opinion.

Q: Douglas MacArthur's role in the Bonus Army (1932) incident was:
1: Refusing Hoover's order to use force and resigning in protest
2: Commanding the military operation that forcibly dispersed the veterans, exceeding Hoover's orders in his aggressiveness
3: Testifying before Congress that military force had been necessary and proportionate
4: Negotiating a peaceful settlement that gave veterans partial bonus payment
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hoover ordered MacArthur to clear specific encampments; MacArthur exceeded these orders, driving veterans entirely from Washington using cavalry, bayonets, and tear gas. The spectacle of an American general attacking WWI veterans on American soil cost Hoover enormously in public opinion.

Q: Unemployment during the Great Depression peaked at approximately:
1: 10 percent of the workforce
2: 25 percent of the workforce
3: 17 percent of the workforce
4: 40 percent of the workforce
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: At its peak in 1932–33, roughly one in four American workers was unemployed. Black unemployment was estimated as high as 50%. These figures don't count the millions more who were underemployed — working part-time or far below their previous wages.

Q: The Great Depression only truly ended because of:
1: WWII's massive industrial mobilization, which created full employment through wartime production
2: The banking reforms of the First New Deal, which restored confidence in financial institutions
3: The Second New Deal programs of 1935–36, which finally restored consumer spending
4: The Social Security Act (1935), which put purchasing power directly into elderly Americans' hands
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: By 1940 unemployment was still around 15%. It was wartime spending — on ships, planes, tanks, uniforms, and everything else — that created full employment. The U.S. became the "Arsenal of Democracy," running factories 24 hours a day and actually experiencing labor shortages by 1943.

CHAPTER: 24 — World War II

Q: The Manchurian Incident (September 18, 1931) was historically significant because:
1: Japan staged a railway explosion and used it as a pretext to invade Manchuria, establishing a puppet state
2: It was Japan's first military defeat, which began destabilizing its government
3: It marked the first use of chemical weapons by any Asian military power in the 20th century
4: China and Japan signed a mutual defense treaty that would later pull the U.S. into the Pacific War
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: The Kwantung Army staged a small explosion on a Japanese-owned railway, blamed it on Chinese saboteurs, and used it as justification for occupying all of Manchuria. The League of Nations condemned Japan; Japan simply walked out of the League. The Stimson Doctrine was the U.S. response — refusing to recognize the puppet state of Manchukuo.

Q: The Rape of Nanjing (1937) refers to:
1: Germany's aerial bombing of civilian populations across Southern Europe
2: The Soviet Union's invasion of Finland, which targeted Finnish civilian infrastructure
3: Italy's use of chemical weapons during its invasion of Ethiopia in violation of international law
4: Japan's massacre of an estimated 250,000 to 300,000 Chinese civilians following the fall of China's capital
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: After capturing Nanjing (then China's capital), Japanese forces engaged in six weeks of systematic mass murder, rape, and looting. Estimates of the death toll range from 100,000 to 300,000. The massacre became a defining atrocity in modern memory and a major source of lasting Chinese-Japanese hostility.

Q: The U.S. oil embargo on Japan (1940) escalated tensions because:
1: It was coupled with a naval blockade that prevented Japan from trading with any nation
2: It cut off Japanese exports of raw materials that American factories depended on
3: Japan imported nearly 80% of its oil from the U.S., which viewed the embargo as a de facto declaration of war
4: It demonstrated that the U.S. was abandoning its official neutrality and joining the Allied war effort
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Japan had almost no domestic oil supply. The U.S. embargo of oil, steel, and rubber was economically catastrophic for Japanese military ambitions. Japanese military planners concluded they had two choices: accept American diplomatic demands (including withdrawing from China) or seize the oil fields of the Dutch East Indies by force — which meant war with the U.S.

Q: Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) was historically decisive because:
1: The destruction of the U.S. Pacific Fleet meant Japan now controlled all of the Pacific Ocean
2: FDR used the attack to justify declaring war on both Japan and Germany simultaneously
3: It was the first time in history a naval attack had been launched from aircraft carriers
4: The surprise attack killed 2,400 Americans and immediately brought the U.S. into WWII
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Pearl Harbor killed 2,400 Americans, wounded 1,200 more, and destroyed or damaged much of the Pacific Fleet. FDR called December 7 "a date which will live in infamy." Congress declared war on Japan the next day. Germany and Italy declared war on the U.S. four days later.

Q: Blitzkrieg, the German military strategy, was effective primarily because:
1: It relied on massed artillery barrages that demolished fixed defensive positions
2: It used submarine warfare to cut off enemy supply lines before land invasion
3: It used extensive deception and false intelligence to mislead enemies about the invasion's true direction
4: It combined fast-moving tanks, motorized infantry, and air support to overwhelm defenses before they could respond
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Blitzkrieg ("lightning war") shattered the WWI model of slow, grinding trench warfare. Fast-moving tank columns would punch through enemy lines while dive-bombers attacked communications and supply lines. The speed of collapse — France fell in six weeks — shocked the world.

Q: The Battle of Britain (1940) was historically significant because:
1: British and French ground forces halted Germany's advance through the Low Countries
2: The British RAF's defeat of the German Luftwaffe prevented Hitler from launching an invasion of Britain
3: Allied naval forces sank the German submarine fleet, securing Atlantic supply lines
4: It was the first battle in which radar technology was used, giving Britain a decisive advantage
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hitler's planned invasion of Britain (Operation Sea Lion) required air superiority first. The Luftwaffe's failure to defeat the RAF meant the invasion was postponed indefinitely — Britain remained in the war, providing the essential base from which the eventual Allied liberation of Europe would be launched.

Q: Operation Barbarossa (June 1941) was historically significant primarily because:
1: It opened the North African campaign that would eventually lead Allied forces to invade Italy
2: It was Hitler's massive invasion of the Soviet Union — the largest land invasion in history — that ultimately broke the German army
3: It was the Allied invasion of Southern France designed to draw German forces away from Normandy
4: It established the Eastern Front defensive line that stopped German expansion into the Middle East
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hitler violated the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact to invade the Soviet Union with over 3 million troops. The Soviets suffered catastrophic losses but ultimately stopped and reversed the German advance at enormous cost (25 million Soviet dead). The Eastern Front consumed the bulk of Germany's military strength and was the primary reason Germany lost the war.

Q: D-Day / Operation Overlord (June 6, 1944) is described as the largest amphibious assault in history because:
1: It involved troops from every Allied nation attacking simultaneously across five beaches
2: It required crossing a wider body of water than any previous amphibious operation
3: Over 150,000 Allied troops crossed the English Channel to land at beaches in Normandy, France under Eisenhower's command
4: It was the first amphibious assault planned and executed by an international coalition of nations
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Operation Overlord involved 156,000 troops on the first day alone, supported by 11,000 aircraft and nearly 7,000 naval vessels. Eisenhower commanded. The Normandy breakout led to the liberation of Paris in August 1944 and eventually to Germany's defeat.

Q: The Lend-Lease Act allowed the U.S. to:
1: Draft American men into military service before the U.S. officially entered WWII
2: Impose economic sanctions on Axis nations while maintaining formal neutrality
3: Supply Britain and later the Soviet Union with war materials before the U.S. officially entered the war
4: Seize Axis-owned assets and businesses within the United States
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Rather than sell weapons to Britain (which couldn't afford them), FDR proposed "lending" them, comparing it to lending a garden hose to a neighbor whose house is on fire. Lend-Lease eventually supplied $50 billion in aid to 30+ countries, making the U.S. the "Arsenal of Democracy" even before Pearl Harbor.

Q: Executive Order 9066 (1942) is historically significant because it:
1: Required all German and Italian Americans to register with the federal government
2: Created military tribunals to try civilians suspected of espionage or sabotage
3: Authorized the forced relocation of approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans into internment camps, about two-thirds of whom were U.S. citizens
4: Suspended habeas corpus for all persons in designated military zones on the Pacific Coast
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The internment of Japanese Americans — justified by wartime hysteria and racial prejudice rather than actual evidence of disloyalty — is one of the most significant civil liberties violations in U.S. history. The 1944 Korematsu decision upheld it; the government apologized and paid reparations in 1988.

Q: The Double V Campaign called for:
1: Victory against fascism abroad and victory against racial discrimination and segregation at home
2: Defeating both Japan and Germany before any Allied forces could be demobilized
3: Doubling the number of African Americans allowed to enlist in combat positions
4: A two-front military strategy attacking Germany from both Western and Eastern Europe simultaneously
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: The Double V Campaign, launched by the Pittsburgh Courier in 1942, captured a profound tension: Black Americans were being asked to fight for democracy abroad while being denied it at home. It articulated African Americans' wartime determination to leverage their military service into civil rights gains.

Q: The Tuskegee Airmen's historical significance was:
1: They were the first Black soldiers allowed to serve in combat during WWII
2: They were the first Black military unit to serve on the front lines in the Pacific theater
3: They were Black officers who commanded integrated units in the European theater
4: They were African American pilots who served with such distinction that they helped dismantle arguments for segregating the military
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: The 332nd Fighter Group compiled an extraordinary combat record — escorting bombers with a remarkably low loss rate — directly refuting racist arguments that Black men couldn't be trusted with complex military technology or combat leadership. Their record contributed to Truman's 1948 desegregation of the military.

Q: The Manhattan Project was:
1: The top-secret U.S. program to develop the atomic bomb, directed scientifically by J. Robert Oppenheimer
2: The Allied deception operation that misled Germany about the location of the D-Day landings
3: FDR's program to rebuild New York City infrastructure during the Depression
4: A military industrial complex program to mass-produce tanks and artillery in New York State
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: The Manhattan Project employed over 130,000 people at sites across the U.S. and Canada. Oppenheimer led the scientific effort at Los Alamos, New Mexico. The first test (Trinity, July 1945) succeeded. Truman authorized the bomb's use on Japan in August 1945.

Q: The Battle of Midway (June 1942) was the turning point of the Pacific War because:
1: American forces landed on Japanese soil for the first time, demonstrating U.S. offensive capability
2: U.S. submarines destroyed Japan's oil supply fleet, crippling its ability to fuel future operations
3: The U.S. broke Japanese naval codes and used the intelligence to destroy four Japanese aircraft carriers, eliminating Japan's carrier superiority
4: Japan's failed landing attempt proved it lacked the amphibious capability to threaten Hawaii or the U.S. mainland
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: By cracking Japanese naval codes, American commanders knew Japan's plans and ambushed the attacking fleet. Losing four carriers (Akagi, Kaga, Soryu, Hiryu) — Japan's most powerful offensive weapon — meant Japan could no longer project power across the Pacific. From Midway onward, Japan was strategically on the defensive.

Q: The Holocaust refers to:
1: The Allied strategic bombing campaign that destroyed German industrial centers and killed civilians
2: The Nazi regime's systematic genocide of approximately 6 million Jews and millions of others, including Roma, disabled people, and political opponents
3: The wartime firebombing of Tokyo that killed more civilians than either atomic bombing
4: Japan's use of biological and chemical weapons against Chinese civilian populations throughout WWII
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Holocaust was not a byproduct of war but a deliberate, industrialized genocide. The Nazis built an infrastructure of deportation, forced labor, and extermination camps. Six million Jews — two-thirds of European Jewry — were murdered, along with millions of others deemed undesirable by Nazi ideology.

Q: The Yalta Conference (February 1945) was significant primarily because:
1: It was where Truman, Churchill, and Stalin agreed on the terms for Japan's surrender
2: The Big Three (FDR, Churchill, Stalin) shaped the postwar European order and demanded unconditional German surrender
3: It formally established the United Nations as the successor to the League of Nations
4: It was the conference where the decision to use the atomic bomb on Japan was finalized
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: At Yalta, the Big Three negotiated the postwar division of Europe, agreed on Soviet entry into the Pacific War, and planned the United Nations. The agreements about Eastern Europe would later become a source of Cold War tension as Stalin consolidated control.

Q: The Zoot Suit Riots (1943) revealed what about wartime America?
1: The extent of labor union radicalism in wartime defense industries
2: That Japanese American internment had created dangerous resentment in other minority communities
3: That the federal government's wartime censorship had failed to suppress racial propaganda
4: Deep racial tensions on the American homefront, as white servicemen attacked Latino and Black youth in Los Angeles
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: White sailors and soldiers attacked young Latino and Black men wearing "zoot suits" (a fashion statement that servicemen and some white civilians associated with draft dodging and disrespect). Police arrested the victims, not the attackers. The riots exposed that the war for democracy abroad coexisted with virulent racism at home.

Q: "Rosie the Riveter" symbolized:
1: The over 6 million women who entered industrial war industries during WWII, taking jobs previously reserved for men
2: Women workers who organized unions in wartime defense industries
3: The female nurses who served in combat zones alongside American soldiers
4: The women's suffrage movement that had won the 19th Amendment two decades earlier
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: With 16 million men in military service, American industry needed workers. Women flooded into previously all-male fields — welding, riveting, assembling aircraft. "Rosie" represented both their competence and their patriotism. Most were pushed out of these jobs when men returned after the war.

Q: Harry Truman's most consequential decision as president was:
1: Authorizing the use of atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, ending WWII and shaping the postwar nuclear world
2: Commanding American forces in the final Pacific island campaign
3: Negotiating the final terms of Germany's surrender at Potsdam
4: Creating the Marshall Plan to rebuild postwar European economies
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Truman authorized the bombings after being briefed on the Manhattan Project only upon becoming president (FDR had kept him unaware). The decision remains one of history's most debated — defenders say it saved lives by avoiding an invasion of Japan; critics say it constituted a war crime against civilians and was partly motivated by signaling strength to the Soviet Union.

Q: The Bataan Death March resulted in:
1: The deaths of approximately 10,000 American and Filipino prisoners on an 80-mile forced march under Japanese captivity
2: The Japanese army's first significant military defeat when Filipino guerrillas ambushed the march
3: The liberation of Allied prisoners from the largest Japanese POW camp in the Philippines
4: The decision by FDR to accelerate the Manhattan Project after learning of Japanese atrocities
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: After the Philippines fell in May 1942, Japanese forces force-marched 76,000 American and Filipino prisoners 80 miles in brutal heat with minimal food or water. Prisoners who fell were killed. Deaths estimated at 10,000. It became a symbol of Japanese brutality that fueled American determination in the Pacific.

Q: Dwight D. Eisenhower's primary role in WWII was:
1: Scientific director of the Manhattan Project to develop the atomic bomb
2: Commander of U.S. Pacific forces during the island-hopping campaign toward Japan
3: U.S. representative at all major Allied conferences including Yalta and Potsdam
4: Supreme Allied Commander in Europe, leading the D-Day invasion at Normandy in 1944
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Eisenhower commanded the North African campaign, the invasion of Sicily and Italy, and ultimately Operation Overlord. Managing the competing egos of generals like Patton and Montgomery while coordinating multinational forces required exceptional diplomatic and organizational skill. His success launched his postwar political career.

Q: J. Robert Oppenheimer is historically significant as:
1: The scientific director of the Manhattan Project, regarded as the "father of the atomic bomb"
2: The president who authorized the development of the Manhattan Project
3: The physicist who first theorized that a chain reaction could produce an atomic explosion
4: The general who ordered the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Oppenheimer led the Los Alamos laboratory where the actual bomb was designed and built. Upon witnessing the Trinity test, he famously recalled the Hindu scripture: "Now I become death, the destroyer of worlds." He later opposed the development of the hydrogen bomb and had his security clearance revoked during the McCarthy era.

Q: The Stimson Doctrine (1932) stated that the United States would:
1: Refuse to recognize territories or governments created through Japanese military aggression
2: Impose oil embargoes on any nation that violated international treaties in Asia
3: Reserve the right to intervene militarily to protect American commercial interests in China
4: Provide military aid to any nation in Asia threatened by Japanese aggression
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Secretary of State Henry Stimson announced the U.S. would not recognize the Japanese puppet state of Manchukuo. The doctrine had no enforcement mechanism — but it established the principle of non-recognition of illegally seized territory that became a cornerstone of American foreign policy.

Q: Korematsu v. United States (1944) is a landmark Supreme Court case because it:
1: Upheld the constitutionality of Japanese American internment, a decision later widely considered one of the Court's greatest failures
2: Established that the president must seek congressional authorization before interning American citizens
3: Required the federal government to pay compensation to Japanese Americans for property lost during internment
4: Struck down Japanese American internment as a violation of the 14th Amendment's equal protection clause
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: The 6-3 decision upheld Executive Order 9066 on national security grounds. The dissents by Justices Murphy, Jackson, and Roberts were prophetic — Murphy called it "legalization of racism." The decision was formally overturned in 2018 in Trump v. Hawaii, though by then the U.S. had already apologized and paid reparations in 1988.

Q: V-E Day (May 8, 1945) marks:
1: The day Japan surrendered, ending all fighting in WWII
2: Germany's formal surrender, ending the war in Europe
3: The successful Allied landings at Normandy that opened the Western Front
4: The day the U.S. dropped the atomic bomb on Hiroshima
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: V-E Day (Victory in Europe Day) came after Hitler's death by suicide in his Berlin bunker on April 30, 1945. Germany signed an unconditional surrender on May 7; V-E Day celebrations erupted across Allied nations on May 8. The Pacific War continued until Japan's surrender on V-J Day, August 15, 1945.

Q: The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (August 1939) was significant because:
1: It formally allied Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy against the Western democracies
2: A Nazi-Soviet nonaggression agreement allowed Germany to invade Poland without fear of a Soviet counterattack, igniting WWII in Europe
3: It pledged Soviet military support to France and Britain if Germany attacked them
4: It divided China into German and Soviet spheres of influence
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The pact shocked the world — ideological arch-enemies signing a nonaggression agreement. Secretly, it also divided Eastern Europe between Germany and the Soviet Union. It freed Hitler to attack Poland without a two-front war. Germany and the Soviet Union both invaded Poland; WWII in Europe began September 1, 1939.

Q: Appeasement, Britain and France's 1938 policy, involved:
1: Building up military defenses to deter further German aggression through strength
2: Allowing Hitler to annex Austria and the Sudetenland in hopes of satisfying his territorial ambitions
3: Forming a military alliance with the Soviet Union to encircle Germany
4: Offering Germany economic concessions in exchange for disarmament agreements
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: At the Munich Conference (September 1938), Britain and France agreed to let Hitler take the Czech Sudetenland. Neville Chamberlain returned claiming "peace for our time." Within six months, Germany occupied all of Czechoslovakia, proving that appeasement only emboldened Hitler.

Q: Madame Chiang Kai-shek's significance during WWII was that she:
1: Lobbied Washington for aid to China and served as a highly effective cultural diplomat to the American public
2: Served as China's official representative at the Yalta and Potsdam conferences
3: Commanded Chinese Nationalist military forces against Japanese invasion
4: Negotiated China's formal military alliance with the United States following Pearl Harbor
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Madame Chiang (Soong Mei-ling) made a celebrated tour of the U.S. in 1943, addressing Congress and speaking to massive crowds. Her English fluency, Western education, and Christian faith made her enormously effective at cultivating American support for Nationalist China. FDR was reportedly charmed, though his military advisors were more skeptical of Chiang Kai-shek's actual war effort.

Q: The U.S. emerged from WWII as the world's dominant superpower primarily because:
1: It was the only major economy left largely intact and the only nation possessing atomic weapons
2: Britain and France were exhausted and declined to challenge U.S. leadership
3: The Soviet Union agreed to American leadership in exchange for assistance rebuilding its economy
4: Its military had the largest number of soldiers and the most battle experience of any Allied nation
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Every other major belligerent — Britain, France, Germany, Japan, the Soviet Union — had suffered massive physical destruction. The U.S. had its industrial plant completely untouched, its territory unoccupied, and a monopoly on atomic weapons (until 1949). This was the foundation of postwar American hegemony.
