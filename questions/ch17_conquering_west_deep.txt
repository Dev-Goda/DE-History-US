CHAPTER: Ch. 17 – Conquering the West (Deep)

Q: The Mormon Migration westward was triggered by:
1: A government land grant offering free land in Utah
2: Religious persecution, including the death of Joseph Smith in 1844
3: Economic depression wiping out Mormon farms
4: A Supreme Court ruling banning the Mormon church
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: After Joseph Smith was murdered by a mob in Carthage, Illinois in 1844, Brigham Young led the Mormons out of the U.S. entirely — heading to Mexican territory in the Great Salt Lake basin to escape further persecution.

Q: Brigham Young led Mormons to:
1: Oregon Territory
2: California
3: The Great Salt Lake region of Utah
4: New Mexico Territory
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Young organized the massive Mormon migration to the Great Salt Lake Valley (then part of Mexico). His organizational genius turned a desert into a functioning theocratic community, directing irrigation, city layout, and economic development.

Q: The Mormon settlement in Utah was significant because Young established:
1: A fully democratic government modeled on the Constitution
2: A theocratic governance structure and promoted agricultural development in an arid region
3: A military republic to defend against federal interference
4: A trading post economy connecting California to the East
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Young ran Utah as a religious state — simultaneously religious leader and governor. He directed all development, demonstrating that religious community organization could sustain a large settlement in what had seemed an uninhabitable desert.

Q: The Homestead Act (1862) was significant because it:
1: Gave Native Americans land rights in the West
2: Accelerated western settlement, often at the expense of Native Americans
3: Guaranteed settlers mineral rights on their 160 acres
4: Replaced purchasing public land at auction
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Act brought hundreds of thousands of settlers onto lands Native Americans had been using — accelerating conflict, broken treaties, and forced removal. It fulfilled rapid settlement goals but at enormous human cost.

Q: The Transcontinental Railroad was completed in 1869 at:
1: Denver, Colorado
2: Salt Lake City, Utah
3: Promontory Point, Utah
4: Omaha, Nebraska
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The Union Pacific (building west) and Central Pacific (building east) tracks were joined with the "Golden Spike" at Promontory Summit, Utah on May 10, 1869 — completing coast-to-coast travel in days rather than months.

Q: Which two companies built the Transcontinental Railroad?
1: Pennsylvania Railroad and Baltimore & Ohio
2: Union Pacific and Central Pacific
3: Northern Pacific and Southern Pacific
4: Great Northern and Santa Fe
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Union Pacific built westward from Omaha using mostly Irish immigrant labor; Central Pacific built eastward from Sacramento using primarily Chinese immigrant labor. Both raced to claim more government land grants per mile of track laid.

Q: A major negative consequence of the Transcontinental Railroad was:
1: It caused massive urban unemployment
2: It contributed to the destruction of Native lands and decimation of buffalo populations
3: It led to a financial collapse triggering the Panic of 1873
4: It made the Homestead Act unenforceable
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The railroad brought hunters who killed millions of buffalo (vital to Plains tribes for food, clothing, and shelter), brought settlers onto Native lands, and enabled the Army to move troops rapidly to suppress Native resistance throughout the West.

Q: The Dakota War (1862) was primarily caused by:
1: Gold discovered on Sioux lands
2: Starvation and U.S. government violations of treaties
3: White settlers illegally mining on reservation lands
4: An unprovoked Army attack on a Sioux village
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The U.S. government was chronically late with treaty payments and food rations. A government agent reportedly said "let them eat grass." Starvation combined with treaty violations drove desperate Dakota warriors to attack, triggering a brutal war.

Q: The Dakota War ended with:
1: The first federal treaty guaranteeing Native land rights
2: The largest mass execution in U.S. history — 38 Dakota men hanged
3: The forced removal of all Sioux to Oklahoma
4: The execution of Sitting Bull
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Lincoln approved the execution of 38 Dakota men on December 26, 1862 — the largest mass execution in U.S. history. Hundreds more had been sentenced to death but Lincoln commuted their sentences after personally reviewing the trial records.

Q: The Treaty of Fort Laramie (1868) granted ownership of the Black Hills to:
1: The Cheyenne and Arapaho nations
2: The Sioux Nation
3: The Navajo people
4: The Comanche and Kiowa tribes
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The treaty guaranteed the Great Sioux Reservation, including the sacred Black Hills (Paha Sapa), as permanent Sioux territory. The U.S. violated this within six years when Custer's 1874 expedition confirmed gold in the Black Hills.

Q: Why was the Treaty of Fort Laramie violated?
1: The Sioux attacked neighboring tribes, voiding it
2: Congress abolished all previous Native treaties
3: Gold was discovered in the Black Hills
4: The Sioux refused to allow a railroad through their territory
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: After Custer's 1874 expedition announced gold in the Black Hills, thousands of miners flooded in illegally. The U.S. offered to buy the Hills; when the Sioux refused, the government effectively abandoned treaty obligations and war followed.

Q: The Sand Creek Massacre (1864) involved militia attacking:
1: A Lakota Sioux war camp preparing to raid settlers
2: A peaceful Cheyenne and Arapaho camp, killing mostly women and children
3: A Navajo village along the Long Walk route
4: A Dakota band that had fled after the 1862 uprising
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Colonel Chivington attacked a camp flying both a U.S. flag and a white flag of peace. Over 150 were killed, mostly women and children. Chivington's troops took body parts as trophies. Congress later condemned the attack as a massacre.

Q: What context made Sand Creek more likely?
1: The Civil War diverting federal troops
2: The Colorado gold rush creating settler pressure on Native lands
3: A series of Cheyenne raids on army depots
4: President Lincoln's order to clear Colorado of Native people
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Colorado gold rush brought massive settler pressure onto Cheyenne and Arapaho lands. Territorial governor Evans wanted the land open for settlement and encouraged militia commander Chivington's violent approach to "removing" Native peoples.

Q: Sitting Bull was significant because:
1: He was the first Native American to serve in Congress
2: He led the forced relocation of the Navajo during the Long Walk
3: He played a crucial role in defeating Custer at Little Bighorn and became a symbol of Native resistance
4: He negotiated the Treaty of Fort Laramie
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Sitting Bull was both military and spiritual leader — his vision that warriors would defeat soldiers "falling into camp like grasshoppers" inspired the Little Bighorn battle. He later toured with Buffalo Bill's Wild West show before being killed in 1890.

Q: The Battle of Little Bighorn (1876) was significant because:
1: It was the last Native American victory
2: It was a temporary Native victory, after which the U.S. retaliated more harshly
3: It resulted in a treaty granting Native Americans full citizenship
4: It convinced Congress to immediately pass the Dawes Act
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The overwhelming Lakota and Cheyenne victory shocked the nation. Congress responded by dramatically increasing army funding and operations against Plains tribes — Little Bighorn was a tactical win for Native Americans but a strategic disaster accelerating their defeat.

Q: Crazy Horse is remembered as:
1: The leader who negotiated peace with the U.S. after Little Bighorn
2: A symbol of Native American resistance and bravery against U.S. expansion
3: The leader who surrendered to Custer before the battle
4: The spiritual leader who introduced the Ghost Dance
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Crazy Horse refused to live on a reservation and led warriors with legendary skill. After surrendering in 1877 due to starvation, he was killed while in Army custody under disputed circumstances, becoming a martyr figure of the resistance.

Q: The "Long Walk" (1864) refers to:
1: The forced march of Cherokee people from Georgia to Oklahoma
2: The voluntary Mormon migration from Illinois to Utah
3: The forced relocation of the Navajo to the Bosque Redondo reservation in New Mexico
4: The route of Sherman's March to the Sea
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Kit Carson's campaign burned Navajo crops and destroyed their herds, forcing surrender through starvation. The 300-mile forced march to a desolate New Mexico reservation killed hundreds. The Navajo were allowed to return to their homeland in 1868.

Q: Thousands of Navajo suffered during the Long Walk primarily from:
1: Armed resistance by U.S. cavalry along the route
2: Starvation and exposure during the journey
3: Forced labor in New Mexico silver mines
4: Disease outbreaks from contact with settlers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Bosque Redondo reservation had poor soil, bad water, and inadequate government supplies. Hundreds died during the march and thousands more suffered at the reservation before the 1868 treaty allowed return to their homeland.

Q: The Dawes Act was intended to:
1: Give Native Americans more land and economic independence
2: Assimilate Native Americans by making them individual landowners like Euro-Americans
3: Prepare reservation lands for government mining
4: Return previously stolen land to Native families
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Dawes Act's architects believed individual land ownership and farming would "civilize" Native Americans. In practice it stripped collective land ownership, a foundation of Native culture, and transferred 90 million acres to whites as "surplus" land.

Q: The Ghost Dance movement promised:
1: Full legal citizenship and voting rights
2: U.S. government payments for lost lands
3: The return of buffalo, ancestors, and disappearance of white settlers
4: Safe passage to Canada
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The Ghost Dance emerged during desperate poverty on reservations. It spread rapidly because it offered hope — spiritual power that could restore everything taken by U.S. expansion. The government viewed it as a threat and suppressed it, leading to the Wounded Knee Massacre.

Q: Turner's Frontier Thesis argued:
1: The frontier was conquered mainly through military force
2: The frontier experience shaped American democracy, independence, and national character
3: The U.S. must find a new frontier overseas through imperialism
4: The frontier's closure would lead to democratic decline
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Turner argued that unlike Europe (where democracy had to be won from aristocracies), American democracy grew from the frontier — the constant challenge of wilderness created self-reliant, egalitarian individuals who built democratic institutions from scratch.

Q: Turner's Frontier Thesis has been mainly criticized for:
1: Underestimating the military's role
2: Overemphasizing railroads in settlement
3: Neglecting the experiences of Native Americans, women, and minorities
4: Arguing the frontier had no influence on American identity
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The "frontier" was not empty land — it was already occupied. Turner's thesis treated Native Americans as obstacles rather than people, and entirely erased women, Mexican Americans, and Chinese immigrants from the story of western development.

Q: Buffalo Bill's Wild West show contributed to:
1: Accurate historical education about frontier conflicts
2: The romanticized and mythologized image of the American West
3: Growing support for Native American rights
4: Opposition to westward expansion
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Buffalo Bill's show featured reenacted battles and Native Americans performing their own "defeat" for paying audiences. It created the enduring mythic West — heroic cowboys, savage enemies, triumphant civilization — that shaped American culture for generations.

Q: Wild West shows reflected anxieties about:
1: Rising immigration from Southern Europe
2: The growth of industrial monopolies
3: Changing ideas about masculinity and fear that frontier culture was disappearing
4: The increasing power of labor unions
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: By the 1880s-90s, many men worked in offices and factories rather than farms or frontiers. Wild West shows offered nostalgic performances of rugged masculine adventure for audiences anxious that industrialization was making American men "soft."
