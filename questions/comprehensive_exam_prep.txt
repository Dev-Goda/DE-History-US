CHAPTER: Ch. 16 – Capital & Labor: Events & Identification

Q: What was the Haymarket Affair?
1: A peaceful rally where workers voted to form the AFL
2: A bombing at a Chicago labor rally that led to the trial and execution of anarchists and severely damaged the Knights of Labor
3: A government program to arbitrate labor disputes in the railroad industry
4: A congressional investigation into monopoly practices at Carnegie Steel
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: In 1886 a bomb exploded at a labor rally in Haymarket Square, Chicago. Eight anarchists were tried — most with little evidence. The association of labor unions with violence destroyed public support for the Knights of Labor almost overnight.

Q: What was the Pullman Strike of 1894?
1: A wildcat strike by coal miners in Pennsylvania that spread to 12 states
2: A nationwide railroad strike triggered by wage cuts at the Pullman Palace Car Company, broken by federal troops
3: A successful strike that won the eight-hour workday for all railroad workers
4: A strike organized by the AFL against Carnegie Steel
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: When Pullman cut wages without cutting rents in his company town, workers struck. The American Railway Union under Eugene Debs joined in solidarity, halting rail traffic nationally. President Cleveland sent federal troops to break it — Debs was jailed and organized labor was crushed again.

Q: What was the Homestead Strike?
1: A strike by tenant farmers demanding lower rents from railroad-owned land
2: A violent 1892 steel strike at Carnegie's Homestead plant where Pinkerton guards clashed with workers
3: A peaceful demonstration that led to the eight-hour workday law
4: A strike by homesteaders protesting railroad land grants
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Carnegie cut wages at his Homestead, PA steel plant. Workers struck. Carnegie hired 300 Pinkerton detectives who arrived by barge — a battle broke out, killing workers and Pinkertons. The state militia restored order, the union was crushed. Carnegie avoided public blame by conveniently being in Scotland.

Q: What was the Great Railroad Strike of 1877?
1: The first national strike in U.S. history, triggered by railroad wage cuts, which spread across the country and was suppressed by state and federal troops
2: A successful strike that forced railroads to recognize the Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers
3: A strike organized by the Populist Party against the railroad monopolies
4: A regional strike in the South protesting railroad segregation policies
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: When the Baltimore & Ohio Railroad cut wages by 10%, workers walked out spontaneously. The strike spread to 11 states — the first true national strike. State militias and federal troops broke it violently. It shocked the country and showed both the power and the vulnerability of labor.

Q: Which of the following BEST describes Social Darwinism as applied to the Gilded Age economy?
1: A scientific theory about animal evolution applied to factory safety regulations
2: The belief that economic competition naturally selected the "fittest" individuals for wealth, and that helping the poor interfered with natural progress
3: A government program to train workers for industrial jobs
4: A religious doctrine arguing that God rewards hard work with wealth
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Herbert Spencer coined "survival of the fittest" and applied Darwin's ideas to human society. For Gilded Age industrialists, Social Darwinism was politically perfect: it said inequality was natural, poverty was deserved, and regulation was harmful — all of which conveniently protected their wealth.

Q: What was the Gospel of Wealth?
1: A religious movement that encouraged workers to accept poverty as God's will
2: Andrew Carnegie's argument that the wealthy had a duty to use their fortunes for the public good through philanthropy
3: A Populist pamphlet arguing that silver coinage would bring prosperity to farmers
4: A Social Darwinist tract arguing that wealth proved moral superiority
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Carnegie argued that great wealth, accumulated through talent and effort, should be returned to society through libraries, universities, and cultural institutions — not passed to heirs. Critics noted this was more comfortable than paying workers living wages in the first place.

Q: The Omaha Platform was:
1: A railroad company's price-fixing agreement that angered farmers
2: The founding document of the Populist Party, adopted in 1892, calling for nationalized railroads, free silver, and a subtreasury system
3: A peace treaty between Native American tribes and the U.S. government
4: A Supreme Court ruling upholding railroad regulation
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Written in 1892 in Omaha, Nebraska, this was the Populist Party's manifesto. It diagnosed the Gilded Age's core problem — concentrated corporate power — and proposed government solutions: nationalize railroads, reform money supply, and give farmers access to credit. Many ideas survived into the Progressive Era.

Q: What was the subtreasury system proposed by Populists?
1: A plan to replace the Treasury Department with a network of regional banks
2: A system allowing farmers to store crops in federal warehouses and borrow money against them at low interest, avoiding exploitative middlemen
3: A proposal to coin silver freely to inflate the money supply
4: A government buyout of all private railroad companies
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Farmers were trapped — they had to sell at harvest when prices were lowest, and borrow at high interest from banks that could foreclose. The subtreasury system would let them store grain and get loans, holding crops until prices rose. It was practical, specific, and decades ahead of its time.

Q: What was the significance of Andrew Carnegie in the Gilded Age?
1: He was the founder of Standard Oil who used horizontal integration to monopolize the oil industry
2: He was a Scottish immigrant who built the largest steel empire in the world through vertical integration and became both celebrated for philanthropy and condemned for labor practices
3: He was the political boss of New York City who controlled immigrant votes through Tammany Hall
4: He was the president who broke up railroad monopolies using the Sherman Antitrust Act
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Carnegie controlled everything from iron ore mines to steel mills to railroads — vertical integration. He became the world's richest man. His Gospel of Wealth and libraries made him beloved publicly. His use of Pinkerton agents and wage cuts made him despised by workers. He is the perfect symbol of the Gilded Age's contradictions.

Q: What was the Sherman Antitrust Act?
1: A law that banned all forms of corporate mergers and acquisitions
2: The first federal law attempting to break up monopolies by prohibiting business combinations that restrained trade — though rarely enforced against corporations in the Gilded Age
3: A law requiring railroads to charge uniform rates to all shippers
4: A law establishing the Interstate Commerce Commission to regulate railroad prices
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Passed in 1890, the Sherman Act was the first federal attempt to limit monopoly power. But courts consistently interpreted it narrowly against corporations and broadly against labor unions (as "combinations in restraint of trade"). It was mostly symbolic until Teddy Roosevelt used it aggressively after 1901.

CHAPTER: Ch. 16 – Capital & Labor: Cause & Effect

Q: What was a direct effect of the Haymarket Affair on organized labor?
1: It led to the formation of the AFL as a replacement for the Knights of Labor
2: It caused public sympathy to shift against labor unions, leading to the rapid decline of the Knights of Labor
3: It prompted Congress to pass the first federal eight-hour workday law
4: It forced Carnegie and Rockefeller to recognize unions at their companies
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The bombing — whether or not union members were responsible — was used to paint all organized labor as dangerous and anarchist. The Knights of Labor, already weakened, never recovered. The AFL's more conservative "bread and butter" unionism was partly a response to learning this lesson.

Q: What was a major CAUSE of the rise of the Populist Party in the 1880s-90s?
1: Urban workers angry about immigration were seeking political representation
2: Farmers facing falling crop prices, high railroad rates, and tight credit from banks felt squeezed by industrial capitalism with no political voice
3: Southern plantation owners seeking to restore the pre-Civil War economic order
4: Religious organizations opposed to Social Darwinism sought political reform
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Farmers saw a brutal squeeze: railroad monopolies charged whatever they wanted to transport crops, banks charged high interest, and crop prices kept falling as productivity increased. The Grange and Farmers' Alliance tried cooperative solutions. When those failed, farmers turned to politics — creating the Populist Party.

Q: McKinley's victory in 1896 had what long-term effect on American politics?
1: It led to the immediate passage of antitrust legislation
2: It ended agrarian Populism as a political force and confirmed that future reform would be urban, middle-class, and Progressive rather than rural and radical
3: It caused the Republican Party to split between conservatives and reformers
4: It directly caused the Spanish-American War by shifting foreign policy toward expansion
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: After 1896, no agrarian radical movement ever again came close to national power. The Populists fused with Democrats and lost badly. The next reform wave — Progressivism — came from middle-class urban reformers who accepted capitalism and sought to regulate it, not replace it.

Q: What was the effect of interchangeable parts and Taylorism on American workers?
1: Workers earned higher wages because their individual skill was more valuable
2: Work became faster, more repetitive, and more controlled by management, reducing skilled artisan jobs and increasing factory discipline
3: Unions became more powerful because workers could be more easily replaced
4: Working conditions improved as efficiency reduced the number of hours required
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Taylorism broke every task into its smallest components, timed it, and eliminated inefficiency — meaning worker autonomy, craft pride, and control over the pace of work. Combined with interchangeable parts, it created the assembly-line model that deskilled labor and made workers more replaceable, weakening their bargaining power.

CHAPTER: Ch. 17 – Conquering the West: Events & Identification

Q: What was the Dawes Act of 1887?
1: A law giving Native Americans full U.S. citizenship and voting rights
2: A law that broke up tribal reservations into individual 160-acre allotments for Native families, with remaining land sold to white settlers
3: A law establishing permanent reservation boundaries that could not be changed without tribal consent
4: A law banning Native Americans from practicing their traditional religions
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Dawes Act was devastating to Native peoples. It destroyed communal land ownership (central to most tribal cultures), gave each family a small plot, and sold the vast "surplus" to white settlers. Between 1887 and 1934, Native Americans lost about 90 million of their 138 million acres.

Q: What was the Ghost Dance movement?
1: A war dance performed by Sioux warriors before the Battle of Little Bighorn
2: A spiritual movement among Plains tribes prophesying that prayer and ritual dancing would bring back the buffalo, return the ancestors, and cause white settlers to disappear
3: A cultural revival movement among Pueblo peoples in the Southwest
4: A secret society among Cherokee leaders planning military resistance
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Ghost Dance spread rapidly in the late 1880s because it offered desperate hope to peoples whose world had been destroyed — buffalo gone, land taken, children in boarding schools. The U.S. government saw it as threatening and banned it. Tensions over the Ghost Dance contributed directly to the Wounded Knee Massacre (1890).

Q: What was the Battle of Little Bighorn?
1: The final U.S. Army victory over the Apache in the Southwest
2: An 1876 battle where Sioux and Cheyenne warriors under Sitting Bull and Crazy Horse annihilated Lt. Col. Custer and his cavalry unit
3: A Comanche ambush of a wagon train in Texas that triggered the Red River War
4: The U.S. Army's final defeat of the Lakota Sioux, ending the Plains Indian Wars
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: "Custer's Last Stand" was a stunning Native American victory — but ultimately a temporary one. The U.S. Army responded with overwhelming force, ultimately confining the Sioux to reservations. Little Bighorn showed both the military capability of Plains tribes and the determination of the U.S. to eliminate their resistance.

Q: What was the Wounded Knee Massacre?
1: The opening battle of the Indian Wars, fought in 1862 in Minnesota
2: An 1890 massacre where U.S. troops killed approximately 250-300 Lakota men, women, and children who had gathered for the Ghost Dance
3: A battle where Nez Perce warriors defeated a U.S. cavalry unit in Oregon
4: A violent clash between homesteaders and Sioux warriors over Homestead Act land
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: U.S. soldiers surrounded Lakota Ghost Dancers at Wounded Knee Creek on the Pine Ridge Reservation. When a shot rang out — from whom is disputed — the army opened fire with rifles and artillery. Most of the dead were unarmed civilians. Wounded Knee is widely considered the final massacre of the Indian Wars.

Q: Who was Sitting Bull?
1: The Apache leader who led a guerrilla resistance campaign in the Southwest for decades
2: A Hunkpapa Lakota chief and spiritual leader who resisted U.S. expansion, helped lead the victory at Little Bighorn, and was later killed during his arrest amid Ghost Dance tensions
3: A Nez Perce chief who led his people on an epic 1,170-mile retreat toward Canada before surrendering
4: A Cherokee leader who negotiated the Trail of Tears agreement with Andrew Jackson
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Sitting Bull was both a political and spiritual leader — rare in Lakota culture. He refused to surrender land in the Black Hills, joined the resistance at Little Bighorn, fled to Canada, and later performed in Buffalo Bill's Wild West Show. He was killed in 1890 by Indian police during tensions around the Ghost Dance.

Q: Who was Chief Joseph?
1: The Sioux chief who organized the resistance at Wounded Knee
2: The Nez Perce chief who led his people on a 1,170-mile retreat across Idaho and Montana trying to reach Canada before surrendering, famous for his speech "I will fight no more forever"
3: The Cherokee chief who signed the Treaty of New Echota leading to the Trail of Tears
4: The Apache leader who evaded U.S. forces for decades in the Arizona desert
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: When the U.S. tried to force the Nez Perce onto a reservation, Chief Joseph led 700 people — mostly women, children, and elderly — in a brilliant fighting retreat. Stopped just 40 miles from the Canadian border after months, he surrendered with one of history's most eloquent speeches.

Q: What was Turner's Frontier Thesis?
1: A congressional report arguing the frontier needed military protection from Native Americans
2: Historian Frederick Jackson Turner's 1893 argument that the unique democratic character of American civilization was shaped by the experience of a moving frontier
3: A Supreme Court decision limiting federal power over territorial expansion
4: A Populist document arguing the frontier should be preserved as public land
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Turner argued that the frontier — cheap land, individualism, constant reinvention — was what made Americans different from Europeans. He presented this in 1893, the same year the Census declared the frontier closed, making it simultaneously a celebration and an elegy. His thesis dominated American historical thinking for decades, despite erasing Native peoples entirely.

Q: What was the significance of Chicago as a railroad hub?
1: Chicago was where all transcontinental railroads were built and managed from
2: Chicago's central location made it the convergence point of the national rail network, which turned it into the nation's largest meatpacking city and a major industrial and commercial center
3: Chicago was chosen as the railroad hub by Congress as part of the Pacific Railroad Act
4: Chicago became a hub only after New York declined due to labor strikes
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Railroads from every direction converged on Chicago — cattle from Texas, grain from the Plains, manufactured goods from the East. This made it the perfect location for meatpacking: cattle walked in, processed meat shipped out on refrigerated rail cars. Chicago's rise is inseparable from the railroad revolution.

Q: What was the significance of Buffalo Bill's Wild West Show?
1: It was a documentary traveling exhibition that accurately depicted frontier life for Eastern audiences
2: It was a hugely popular entertainment spectacle that manufactured romantic myths about the frontier — presenting cowboys, Native Americans, and frontier violence as thrilling entertainment rather than historical reality
3: It was a political organization that lobbied Congress for more westward expansion funding
4: It was a Native American cultural preservation project endorsed by the Bureau of Indian Affairs
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Buffalo Bill Cody's show featured Sitting Bull, Annie Oakley, and reenacted battles. It played to massive audiences in the U.S. and Europe. The show created the cowboy mythology that shaped American identity — brave white heroes, savage or noble Native Americans — a mythology that shaped how Americans understood the West for generations.

CHAPTER: Ch. 17 – Conquering the West: Cause & Effect

Q: What was a major cause of the near-extinction of the American bison by the 1880s?
1: Disease brought by cattle from Mexico spread to bison herds and wiped them out
2: Commercial hunters, encouraged by the U.S. Army, systematically slaughtered bison both for profit and as a deliberate strategy to destroy the Plains tribes' food supply and way of life
3: The expansion of cattle ranching drove bison off their grazing lands
4: Drought across the Great Plains destroyed bison food sources, causing starvation
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Bison were the economic, spiritual, and nutritional foundation of Plains cultures. Killing bison destroyed the economic base of resistance. General Sheridan reportedly said hunters did more to solve the "Indian problem" than the Army could in 30 years. It was a deliberate strategy of cultural and physical destruction.

Q: What was the effect of the Dawes Act on Native American land ownership?
1: It strengthened tribal sovereignty by giving individual families legal title to land
2: It reduced Native American land holdings by about 90 million acres as "surplus" land was sold to white settlers and tribal communal ownership was destroyed
3: It had little practical effect because most tribes refused to participate
4: It was ruled unconstitutional within 20 years and tribal lands were restored
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Dawes Act (1887) was one of the most destructive pieces of legislation in Native American history. By 1934 when it was repealed, Native peoples had lost nearly 2/3 of their remaining land. Communal land ownership — essential to tribal culture and governance — was also deliberately destroyed.

Q: The construction of the transcontinental railroad had what effect on Native American peoples of the Great Plains?
1: It created jobs for Native Americans and improved their economic conditions
2: It brought floods of settlers and hunters, bisected bison migration routes, and accelerated the destruction of Plains cultures by making military campaigns easier to supply
3: It gave tribes access to eastern markets for the first time, strengthening their economies
4: It had little effect on Plains tribes since the railroad ran through already-settled territory
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The railroad was a weapon against Plains cultures as much as an economic tool. It brought settlers to homestead, hunters to slaughter bison, and soldiers to fight. It divided the Great Plains in half, cutting bison migration. In a decade after completion (1869), the Plains tribes were broken and confined to reservations.

CHAPTER: Ch. 18 – Industrial America: Events & Identification

Q: Who was Ida B. Wells?
1: A settlement house founder who created Hull House in Chicago to serve immigrant communities
2: A Black journalist and activist from Memphis who led the first major national campaign against lynching, documenting hundreds of murders and challenging the justifications used to excuse them
3: A muckraking journalist who exposed Standard Oil's monopolistic practices in a famous series of articles
4: A women's rights activist who argued in "Women and Economics" that economic independence was essential to women's liberation
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: After three of Wells' friends were lynched in Memphis in 1892, she investigated lynchings systematically, disproving the "rape" justification that white Southerners used. Her work was courageous — her newspaper office was destroyed and she received death threats. She is one of the most important journalists in American history.

Q: What was the Lost Cause ideology?
1: A Populist movement arguing that farmers had "lost" their economic independence to industrial capitalism
2: A post-Civil War Southern mythology that romanticized the Confederacy, portrayed the war as a noble defense of states' rights rather than slavery, and honored Confederate generals as heroes
3: A labor movement arguing that workers had lost their rights in the industrial era
4: A Native American spiritual movement mourning the loss of traditional lands and way of life
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Lost Cause was powerful propaganda. It reframed the Civil War as a constitutional argument about states' rights, erased slavery as the central cause, and turned Lee, Jackson, and Davis into heroic martyrs. Confederate monuments, textbooks, and organizations spread this mythology nationwide — shaping how generations understood the war.

Q: Who was Charlotte Perkins Gilman?
1: A settlement house founder who established Hull House in Chicago
2: A feminist writer and economist who argued in "Women and Economics" that women's economic dependence on men was the root of their oppression, and who wrote the famous short story "The Yellow Wallpaper"
3: A labor organizer who founded the Women's Trade Union League
4: A journalist who exposed child labor practices in American factories
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Gilman was a major intellectual of the Progressive Era. "Women and Economics" (1898) made the radical argument that until women were economically independent, true equality was impossible. "The Yellow Wallpaper" dramatized how medical establishment treated women's mental health — through control and isolation — as patriarchal oppression.

Q: What was "Muscular Christianity"?
1: A Catholic doctrine promoting physical penance as a form of worship
2: A late 19th-century Protestant movement that equated physical fitness and athletic strength with moral virtue and Christian manliness
3: A Social Gospel movement arguing that Christianity required physical labor in poor communities
4: A YMCA program that built gymnasiums in immigrant neighborhoods
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Muscular Christianity arose partly from anxiety about whether American men were becoming "soft" in industrial, urban life. Leaders like Theodore Roosevelt embodied it — the strenuous life. It fueled the YMCA movement, college athletics, and even attitudes toward imperialism (strong men conquering weak peoples).

Q: What were mutual-aid societies, and what purpose did they serve for immigrants?
1: Government agencies that helped immigrants find factory jobs and housing
2: Community organizations formed by immigrant groups that provided insurance, loans, help during illness, funeral expenses, and social support — essentially a private safety net when no government one existed
3: Religious organizations that required immigrants to give up their native languages to receive aid
4: Labor unions specifically organized for immigrant workers in industrial cities
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Without Social Security, unemployment insurance, or Medicare, immigrants faced catastrophic risk from injury, illness, or death. Mutual-aid societies pooled resources within ethnic communities — Irish, Italian, Polish, Jewish societies all formed. They kept cultures alive, supported members, and were a foundation of immigrant political power.

Q: What was the "New South" movement?
1: A Reconstruction-era federal program to rebuild Southern infrastructure after the Civil War
2: A post-Reconstruction ideology promoted by Southern business leaders who wanted to industrialize the South while maintaining racial hierarchy and white supremacy
3: A Black political movement arguing for a separate Black economic zone in the South
4: A Populist movement arguing for agrarian reform and racial equality in the South
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: "New South" boosters like Henry Grady argued the South should embrace industry, attract Northern investment, and modernize. But this vision never challenged white supremacy — it relied on cheap Black labor kept cheap through terror and disenfranchisement. The South remained mostly agricultural, and Black Southerners remained systematically oppressed.

Q: What were poll taxes and literacy tests, and what was their purpose?
1: Fair civic requirements designed to ensure an educated and responsible electorate
2: Legal tools used by Southern states specifically designed to prevent Black Americans from voting, despite the 15th Amendment theoretically guaranteeing them that right
3: Federal requirements established during Reconstruction to ensure proper voter registration
4: Progressive Era reforms aimed at reducing corruption in urban machine politics
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The 15th Amendment said you couldn't deny voting rights based on race — so Southern states invented race-neutral mechanisms that targeted Black voters in practice. Poll taxes excluded the poor; literacy tests were administered selectively and impossibly to Black applicants; violence backed all of it up. This system lasted until the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

Q: Who was Booker T. Washington, and what was his philosophy?
1: A radical Black activist who argued for immediate full equality and founded the NAACP
2: A formerly enslaved educator who founded Tuskegee Institute and argued that Black Americans should focus on vocational self-improvement and economic success before pursuing political rights
3: A journalist who documented lynching and led the anti-lynching campaign nationally
4: A Reconstruction-era congressman who championed the 14th Amendment
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Washington's Atlanta Compromise (1895) urged Black Americans to accept segregation temporarily, focus on economic self-sufficiency through skilled trades, and eventually earn equality. His critics, especially W.E.B. Du Bois, argued this accommodationism surrendered political rights that were being violently stripped away and made equality even harder to win.

CHAPTER: Ch. 18 – Industrial America: Cause & Effect

Q: What was the primary cause of rapid urbanization in the United States between 1870 and 1920?
1: Declining farm productivity that pushed farmers off their land
2: Industrial job growth in cities attracted both internal migrants (including Black Southerners) and massive waves of immigrants from Europe
3: Government programs that paid people to relocate to cities for factory work
4: Religious revivals that drew people to city churches and settlement houses
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Cities grew because jobs grew — steel, meatpacking, textiles, railroads all needed workers. Immigrants came from Southern and Eastern Europe. Black Southerners began the Great Migration north. By 1920, for the first time, more Americans lived in cities than rural areas — a transformation driven almost entirely by industrial employment.

Q: What was a major effect of the end of Reconstruction on Black Americans in the South?
1: Black Americans gained more economic freedom even if political rights were restricted
2: The withdrawal of federal troops and enforcement allowed white supremacists to use violence, disenfranchisement laws, and economic coercion to systematically reverse Black political gains and impose a racial caste system
3: Most Black Southerners voluntarily moved North to avoid political conflict
4: The Supreme Court protected Black civil rights even after troops were withdrawn
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The 1877 Compromise ended Reconstruction and withdrew federal troops. With no enforcement of the 14th and 15th Amendments, white Southerners used terror and law to dismantle Black political power. By 1900, virtually no Black Americans voted in the Deep South. The gains of Reconstruction were almost entirely reversed in 20 years.

Q: What caused the Tainted Money Debate in American religious life?
1: Questions about whether churches should accept money raised through gambling or alcohol
2: A public controversy over whether religious institutions should accept large donations from industrialists like Rockefeller whose wealth was built on monopoly, wage exploitation, and crushed competition
3: A debate over whether immigrant churches should receive federal funding
4: A controversy over whether labor unions should fund churches in immigrant communities
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Washington Gladden coined "tainted money" when Rockefeller donated $100,000 to the Congregational Mission Board. Could the church accept money from someone who had broken competitors through predatory pricing and exploited workers? The debate forced churches to confront whether accepting philanthropy legitimized injustice.

CHAPTER: Ch. 19 – American Empire: Events & Identification

Q: What was the Spanish-American War?
1: A conflict between Spain and the United States over trade rights in the Caribbean, resolved through negotiation
2: An 1898 war in which the U.S. defeated Spain, gained Guam, Puerto Rico, and the Philippines, and established a protectorate over Cuba — marking America's emergence as a global imperial power
3: A naval war fought primarily in the Atlantic to protect U.S. merchant ships from Spanish pirates
4: A war fought at Spain's request to help suppress a Cuban rebellion that threatened U.S. investments
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The war lasted just 10 weeks. Spain was a weakened empire; the U.S. was an industrial giant looking for markets and strategic bases. The U.S. gained an overseas empire almost overnight. Anti-imperialists like Mark Twain were horrified; expansionists like Theodore Roosevelt were thrilled. The world had a new imperial power.

Q: Who was Alfred Thayer Mahan, and why was he significant?
1: The general who led U.S. forces in the Philippines and later became president
2: A naval officer whose book "The Influence of Sea Power upon History" argued that national greatness required a powerful navy and overseas bases — providing the intellectual framework for American imperialism
3: The Secretary of State who negotiated the purchase of Alaska from Russia
4: A journalist whose "yellow journalism" helped push the U.S. into the Spanish-American War
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Mahan's 1890 book changed how American leaders thought about power. He argued that history proved strong navies built empires — Britain was the model. To become great, the U.S. needed a blue-water navy, coaling stations, and overseas bases. Theodore Roosevelt and Henry Cabot Lodge were disciples. Mahan gave imperialism a strategic logic.

Q: What was the Open Door Policy?
1: A U.S. immigration policy that allowed unlimited Chinese immigration in the 1890s
2: A series of notes issued by Secretary of State John Hay in 1899-1900 calling for all major powers to have equal access to trade with China, preventing any single power from monopolizing the Chinese market
3: A trade agreement between the U.S. and Japan that opened Japanese ports to American commerce
4: A policy allowing American missionaries free access to all regions of China
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The U.S. feared European powers and Japan would carve China into exclusive spheres of influence, locking American merchants out. The Open Door notes asked everyone to play fair — and everyone vaguely agreed without really committing. It was diplomatic cleverness that cost nothing but signaled U.S. interest in Asian markets.

Q: What was the Boxer Rebellion?
1: A Japanese military invasion of China that the U.S. helped China resist
2: A Chinese nationalist uprising in 1900 targeting foreign missionaries, diplomats, and businesses, which was suppressed by an international military coalition including the United States
3: A popular revolt in the Philippines against American colonial rule
4: A Chinese labor strike that spread across American railroads in the West
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The "Boxers" (Righteous and Harmonious Fists) attacked foreign presence in China that had humiliated the country for decades. They besieged the foreign legation district in Beijing for 55 days. Eight nations — including the U.S. — sent troops to break the siege. The episode showed both Chinese nationalism and foreign powers' determination to dominate China.

Q: What was the Roosevelt Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine?
1: Roosevelt's declaration that the U.S. would build the Panama Canal to link Atlantic and Pacific fleets
2: Roosevelt's 1904 assertion that the U.S. had the right to intervene militarily in Latin American countries that failed to maintain order or pay their debts to foreign creditors
3: Roosevelt's extension of the Monroe Doctrine to cover U.S. territories in Asia and the Pacific
4: Roosevelt's agreement with European powers to share influence in Latin America
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Monroe Doctrine (1823) said Europe couldn't colonize the Americas. The Roosevelt Corollary flipped this: if Latin American countries couldn't manage their affairs, the U.S. would do it for them — to prevent European intervention. In practice it justified repeated U.S. military intervention in Haiti, Nicaragua, Dominican Republic, and elsewhere.

Q: What was the Guano Islands Act of 1856?
1: A treaty with Spain granting the U.S. the right to harvest resources from Spanish territories
2: A law allowing American citizens to claim uninhabited islands containing guano deposits on behalf of the United States — an early form of informal American territorial expansion
3: An agreement with Britain dividing Pacific islands between the two powers
4: A law regulating the fertilizer trade between the U.S. and South America
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Guano (bird and bat droppings) was the most valuable agricultural fertilizer of the 19th century. The Guano Islands Act authorized Americans to claim any uninhabited island with guano deposits for U.S. jurisdiction. By 1903, the U.S. had claimed about 94 islands this way — an overlooked piece of early American imperial expansion.

Q: Who was William Randolph Hearst, and what role did yellow journalism play in the Spanish-American War?
1: A muckraking journalist who exposed corruption in Standard Oil, indirectly leading to the Spanish-American War
2: A newspaper publisher whose sensationalized reporting on Spanish atrocities in Cuba helped whip up American public opinion in favor of war with Spain
3: A Spanish diplomat whose communications were intercepted and published, triggering the war
4: An anti-imperialist journalist who opposed the Spanish-American War and argued for Cuban independence without U.S. involvement
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hearst's New York Journal and Pulitzer's New York World competed to publish the most dramatic Cuba stories — atrocities, concentration camps, the mysterious sinking of the USS Maine. Whether Hearst really said "You furnish the pictures, I'll furnish the war" is disputed, but yellow journalism undeniably inflamed public sentiment for war.

Q: What was the Philippine-American War?
1: A Spanish attempt to reconquer the Philippines after losing them to the U.S.
2: A brutal guerrilla war from 1899 to 1902 in which Filipino forces under Emilio Aguinaldo fought for independence against U.S. occupation, resulting in over 200,000 Filipino civilian deaths
3: A Japanese invasion of the Philippines that the U.S. helped repel
4: A Filipino uprising against Spanish rule that the U.S. helped suppress
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Filipinos had already been fighting Spain for independence. They thought the U.S. would grant it. When the U.S. kept the Philippines instead, Aguinaldo led a resistance. The U.S. used water torture, burned villages, and put civilians in concentration camps. More Americans died here than in the Spanish-American War — yet few Americans know this war happened.

Q: Who was Emilio Aguinaldo?
1: The Spanish governor of the Philippines who surrendered to Dewey at Manila Bay
2: The Filipino nationalist leader who led the Philippine Revolution against Spain, briefly allied with the U.S., then led the resistance against American occupation when independence was denied
3: The U.S. general who commanded forces during the Philippine-American War
4: A Filipino diplomat who negotiated the Treaty of Paris ceding the Philippines to the U.S.
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Aguinaldo's story is a powerful illustration of American imperial hypocrisy. He led the fight against Spanish colonialism, accepted U.S. support, declared Philippine independence in 1898 — then watched as the U.S. purchased the Philippines from Spain for $20 million and claimed sovereignty. His subsequent resistance was brutally crushed.

CHAPTER: Ch. 19 – American Empire: Cause & Effect

Q: What was the primary motivation for U.S. imperialist expansion in the late 1890s?
1: Religious missionaries lobbied the government to annex territories to spread Christianity
2: A combination of economic interests (new markets and raw materials), strategic military thinking (Mahan's naval theory), Social Darwinist ideology about Anglo-Saxon superiority, and competition with European imperial powers
3: The U.S. government wanted to spread democracy and free elections globally
4: The U.S. needed to reduce its growing population by establishing colonial settlements abroad
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: No single cause explains imperialism — it was a convergence. Industrialists needed markets. The Navy needed bases. Intellectuals provided justification (Mahan, Social Darwinism). Politicians competed with Britain and Germany. Missionaries wanted to "civilize." All of these pressures pushed in the same direction simultaneously.

Q: What was a consequence of the Chinese Exclusion Act?
1: China imposed retaliatory tariffs on American goods, damaging bilateral trade
2: Chinese immigration to the U.S. virtually stopped, Chinese-American communities became more isolated, and it established the precedent of explicitly race-based immigration restriction — eventually extended to other groups
3: Chinese workers were deported from the transcontinental railroad projects
4: It caused the U.S. to lose its Open Door trading rights in China
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act was the first and only law to ban a specific national/ethnic group from immigration. It devastated Chinese-American communities (many could not bring wives or families). It was renewed in 1892, made permanent in 1902, and not fully repealed until 1943. It established racial exclusion as normal immigration policy.

Q: The acquisition of Hawaii had what strategic purpose for the United States?
1: Hawaii's sugar plantations were needed to supply U.S. domestic demand
2: Hawaii provided a mid-Pacific naval base and coaling station essential to U.S. strategic control of Pacific trade routes and military projection toward Asia
3: Hawaii was acquired to prevent Japan from colonizing it
4: Hawaii's location made it a useful diplomatic base for negotiations with China
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Mahan's naval theory made Hawaii's strategic value obvious — it sat in the middle of Pacific trade routes. American sugar planters overthrew Queen Liliuokalani in 1893 with help from U.S. Marines. Cleveland refused annexation as immoral. McKinley annexed it in 1898 during the Spanish-American War when strategic logic was impossible to resist.

Q: What was the effect of the Wilson administration's intervention in Veracruz, Mexico in 1914?
1: It successfully prevented a German arms shipment from reaching Mexico
2: It inflamed Mexican nationalism, damaged U.S.-Mexico relations, and undermined Wilson's stated commitment to self-determination — demonstrating that Progressive presidents were just as willing to use force in Latin America as Roosevelt
3: It led directly to the Mexican-American alliance against Germany in World War I
4: It was welcomed by most Mexicans as protection against Huerta's dictatorial government
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Wilson intervened after Mexican authorities briefly arrested U.S. sailors — hardly a major incident. U.S. forces occupied Veracruz for seven months, killing hundreds of Mexicans. Anti-American sentiment exploded across Latin America. Wilson's moralizing about democracy looked hollow when he was occupying a foreign port city over a minor slight.

CHAPTER: Synthesis – Comparing Events & Themes

Q: Which sequence correctly orders these events from EARLIEST to LATEST?
1: Dawes Act → Haymarket Affair → Spanish-American War → Wounded Knee
2: Haymarket Affair → Dawes Act → Wounded Knee → Spanish-American War
3: Wounded Knee → Dawes Act → Haymarket Affair → Spanish-American War
4: Spanish-American War → Dawes Act → Haymarket Affair → Wounded Knee
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Haymarket (1886) → Dawes Act (1887) → Wounded Knee (1890) → Spanish-American War (1898). This timeline shows how quickly the late 19th century moved: labor crisis, Native dispossession, and imperial expansion all within 12 years.

Q: The Haymarket Affair, Homestead Strike, and Pullman Strike share which common outcome?
1: All three resulted in legislative victories for the labor movement
2: In all three cases federal or state authority intervened on the side of capital against workers, demonstrating that the government would not be a neutral referee in labor disputes
3: All three were organized by the AFL and ultimately strengthened its membership
4: All three led directly to new labor laws protecting workers' rights
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: This pattern is the defining feature of Gilded Age labor relations. The government consistently used its power — troops, injunctions, courts — to break strikes and jail leaders. Workers faced not just employers but the full coercive apparatus of the state. This is why labor in this era was ultimately defeated despite enormous courage and organization.

Q: Social Darwinism, the Lost Cause, and anti-Chinese sentiment all share which underlying function?
1: All three were religious movements that provided moral frameworks for their adherents
2: All three were ideological systems that justified a racial or social hierarchy by naturalizing or romanticizing it — making inequality seem inevitable, deserved, or glorious
3: All three were responses to the threat of labor organizing
4: All three were promoted primarily by Southern Democrats as regional political strategies
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Ideology serves power. Social Darwinism made poverty seem natural. The Lost Cause made white supremacy seem noble. Anti-Chinese racism made exclusion seem logical. Each gave intellectual or emotional cover for treating certain people as less deserving of rights and opportunity. Studying these together reveals how ideology is constructed to justify existing power.

Q: The Populist Party and the Progressive movement were both reform responses to Gilded Age inequality. Their most important DIFFERENCE was:
1: Populists wanted to end capitalism; Progressives wanted to strengthen it
2: Populists were primarily rural farmers who wanted radical systemic change; Progressives were primarily urban middle-class reformers who accepted capitalism and sought to regulate it
3: Populists were racially inclusive; Progressives supported segregation
4: Progressives wanted to nationalize railroads; Populists opposed government ownership
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Populism came from the bottom — people being crushed by the system. Progressivism came from the middle — educated professionals who feared both big business AND radical labor. Progressives wanted efficiency, not revolution. This is why Progressive Era reforms (antitrust, food safety, direct elections) regulated capitalism rather than replacing it.

Q: Which of the following is the BEST example of informal imperialism?
1: Britain colonizing India and directly governing it through the British Raj
2: The U.S. asserting the Open Door Policy in China and the Roosevelt Corollary in Latin America without formally annexing either region
3: Spain controlling Cuba and Puerto Rico as colonial territories
4: Germany establishing a colony in Southwest Africa
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Formal imperialism means direct colonial rule. Informal imperialism means economic dominance and political influence without annexation — cheaper and less controversial. The U.S. was a master of informal imperialism: open the markets, install friendly governments, intervene when needed, but avoid the cost and complications of direct administration.

Q: Reconstruction's failure and the Ghost Dance suppression both illustrate which principle?
1: That minority groups lack the organizational capacity to maintain political gains
2: That legal rights without enforcement mechanisms are hollow — both freed Black Americans and Native peoples won formal protections that were systematically stripped when federal power withdrew or was used against them
3: That religious and spiritual movements cannot sustain political resistance
4: That state governments are inherently more just than the federal government
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: This is one of the most important lessons of this era. The 14th and 15th Amendments were real legal protections — until enforcement ended in 1877. The Ghost Dance was legal religious expression — until the Army declared it threatening. Rights on paper mean nothing without power and willingness to enforce them.

Q: Carnegie's philanthropy and Rockefeller's philanthropy were similar in that both:
1: Came before their industrial fortunes, funded by family wealth
2: Were used to justify enormous wealth built through exploitation — offering the public good things (libraries, universities) while resisting systemic change in wages, working conditions, or market competition
3: Were used to directly fund labor unions and workers' organizations
4: Were required by law under early antitrust regulations
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: This is the "Tainted Money" debate in action. Carnegie built 2,500 libraries. Rockefeller founded the University of Chicago. But both simultaneously crushed unions, bribed politicians, and paid poverty wages. Philanthropy let them write their own legacy without surrendering control over the conditions that generated their wealth.

Q: The muckrakers of the Progressive Era (Ida Tarbell, Upton Sinclair, Lincoln Steffens) and Ida B. Wells shared what approach to reform?
1: All ran for political office to change laws from within the government
2: All used investigative journalism and detailed documentation to expose specific injustices, build public awareness, and create pressure for reform
3: All founded settlement houses to provide direct services to affected communities
4: All worked through the courts to challenge unjust laws through litigation
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The pen as a weapon for reform. Wells documented lynching systematically. Tarbell traced Standard Oil's corruption through financial records. Sinclair went undercover in Chicago meatpacking plants. Steffens documented city machine corruption. All used facts to force the public and politicians to confront what they preferred to ignore.

Q: The settlement house movement and the Farmers' Alliance were similar responses to Gilded Age inequality because:
1: Both sought to end capitalism through radical political organization
2: Both built cooperative community institutions from the bottom up to help members survive economic pressure when neither markets nor government provided protection
3: Both focused primarily on winning voting rights for their members
4: Both were founded by wealthy philanthropists using donations from industrialists
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Jane Addams' Hull House provided English classes, childcare, legal aid, and cultural programs for Chicago immigrants. The Farmers' Alliance built cooperative stores and grain elevators to cut out middlemen. Neither waited for government — both built mutual support structures within communities. This is grassroots organizing at its most practical.

Q: "Yellow journalism," Wild West shows, and the Lost Cause mythology all demonstrate:
1: That Gilded Age Americans were uniquely gullible and easily manipulated
2: That mass media and popular entertainment in the late 19th century powerfully shaped national identity — constructing myths about heroic Americans, dangerous "others," and a romanticized past that served political and economic interests
3: That Southern and Western Americans resisted the cultural changes of industrialization
4: That the federal government used media to promote nationalist ideology
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: All three manufactured stories. Yellow journalism made foreign peoples seem barbaric and war seem glorious. Wild West shows made conquest seem heroic. The Lost Cause made slavery seem benign. Who controls the story controls what people believe about who they are and what their country stands for. The Gilded Age was the birth of modern narrative power.

Q: What do the Homestead Act, the transcontinental railroad, and the Dawes Act have in common?
1: All three were passed during Reconstruction specifically to help freedmen build economic lives
2: All three were federal policies that opened Native American lands to white settlement — directly or indirectly accelerating Native dispossession while appearing to serve other goals
3: All three were championed by the Populist Party as solutions to agrarian poverty
4: All three were declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court within 30 years
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Homestead Act gave settlers 160 acres — of land taken from Native peoples. The railroad cut through treaty lands and brought soldiers. The Dawes Act legally dispossessed remaining tribal lands. Together they represent a comprehensive federal program to transfer land from Native peoples to white settlers, using economic incentives, infrastructure, and law rather than always open violence.

Q: The Dawes Act, Chinese Exclusion Act, Jim Crow laws, and reservation policy all share which characteristic?
1: All four were passed by Southern states as regional measures
2: All four were federal or state policies that used law to enforce racial hierarchy — restricting, confining, or stripping rights from non-white groups in the same era that celebrated American democracy and freedom
3: All four were eventually overturned by the Supreme Court before 1900
4: All four were primarily economic measures with racial effects that were unintended
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The same political culture that built the Statue of Liberty and celebrated freedom also systematically used the law to dispossess Native Americans, exclude Chinese immigrants, strip Black Americans of the vote, and confine minority groups to poverty. The contradiction was not accidental — it was sustained by ideology (Social Darwinism, white supremacy) and political will.

Q: The Open Door Policy, the Monroe Doctrine, and the Roosevelt Corollary together represent what U.S. foreign policy approach?
1: Isolationism — keeping the U.S. out of all foreign conflicts
2: A progression from hemispheric defense to global economic and political dominance — expanding the definition of what the U.S. had the right to control
3: Democratic internationalism — spreading free elections to foreign nations
4: Collective security — building alliances to maintain world peace
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Monroe said "Europe stay out of the Americas." Roosevelt said "If Latin Americans can't manage their affairs, we will." The Open Door said "We expect access to Chinese markets." Together they trace a logic of expansion: from defending the hemisphere, to policing it, to projecting power globally. Each step built on the last.

Q: What was Tammany Hall, and why was it significant?
1: A Progressive Era reform organization that fought municipal corruption in New York City
2: The powerful Democratic political machine that dominated New York City politics, providing immigrants with jobs, services, and social support in exchange for votes — a model of machine politics that critics called corrupt and defenders called practical
3: A settlement house founded by Jane Addams to serve immigrant communities on the Lower East Side
4: The headquarters of the American Federation of Labor under Samuel Gompers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Tammany Hall "bossed" New York politics for over a century. Bosses like William "Boss" Tweed and later Tammany figures got immigrants jobs, helped them with rent, attended their weddings and funerals — in exchange for votes. Critics said it was corruption; defenders said it was the only social safety net available. Both were right.

Q: What was the significance of the 1920 Census result that a majority of Americans lived in cities?
1: It proved that industrialization had failed because it drove people off farms
2: It marked a fundamental transformation of American civilization — the United States had shifted from a rural, agricultural nation (as the founders envisioned) to an urban, industrial one, with profound consequences for politics, culture, and identity
3: It triggered the Populist movement's revival as rural areas sought political protection
4: It led directly to Prohibition as urban reformers sought to control immigrant drinking culture
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Jefferson imagined a republic of independent farmers. By 1920 that vision was dead. Most Americans lived in cities, worked for wages, depended on complex industrial systems, and lived alongside people of different ethnicities and religions. American democracy would have to adapt to serve an urban, industrial, pluralistic society — and much of the 20th century was about figuring out how.

Q: What was the significance of the AFL's "bread and butter" unionism compared to the Knights of Labor's approach?
1: The AFL's approach was more radical and therefore more successful at changing society
2: The AFL's pragmatic focus on wages, hours, and conditions for skilled workers was less idealistic but far more durable — it survived the Gilded Age when broader reform unionism failed, becoming the backbone of American organized labor into the 20th century
3: The AFL's approach was indistinguishable from the Knights of Labor in strategy
4: The AFL's approach succeeded because it included all workers regardless of skill, race, or gender
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Knights tried to organize everyone and transform society. The AFL organized skilled (and mostly white male) workers and focused on immediate gains. The Knights were destroyed; the AFL survived. The lesson Gompers drew: don't threaten the system, just extract better terms from it. This pragmatism defined American unionism for the next 50 years.

Q: Who was Samuel Gompers, and what was his significance?
1: The founder of the Knights of Labor who led the broad reform union movement of the 1870s-80s
2: The founder and long-time president of the AFL who built American trade unionism around practical "bread and butter" issues — wages, hours, conditions — for skilled workers, rejecting socialism and broad social reform
3: The Socialist presidential candidate who ran against Wilson in 1912 and received nearly a million votes
4: The Pullman Strike leader who was jailed for contempt of court and later ran for president from prison
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Gompers ran the AFL from its founding in 1886 until his death in 1924 (except one year). He believed in working within capitalism — not overthrowing it. His approach was "pure and simple" unionism: better wages, shorter hours, better conditions. Less inspiring than the Knights' vision, but AFL unions actually survived the period.

Q: What was the significance of the Interstate Commerce Act of 1887?
1: It successfully broke up all railroad monopolies and restored fair competition
2: It was the first federal law regulating private business (specifically railroads), establishing the Interstate Commerce Commission — a precedent for federal regulation of industry that was weak at first but laid the groundwork for future Progressive Era regulation
3: It nationalized the railroad industry and placed it under government control
4: It gave the federal government power to set railroad wages and working conditions
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The ICC was the first federal regulatory agency. It was supposed to ensure fair railroad rates — but courts consistently ruled against it, making it nearly toothless for 20 years. Its importance was symbolic and structural: it established that the federal government had a role in regulating private industry. Theodore Roosevelt strengthened it dramatically after 1901.

Q: What was the significance of the U.S. Steel Corporation's formation in 1901?
1: It showed that the federal government could successfully regulate big business through antitrust law
2: As the first billion-dollar corporation, financed by J.P. Morgan who bought out Carnegie, it represented the apex of the Gilded Age merger wave and concentrated more productive capacity in a single company than any nation on Earth except the U.S. itself
3: It demonstrated that immigrant workers could rise to control major corporations
4: It was the corporation that Teddy Roosevelt successfully broke up using the Sherman Act
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: When J.P. Morgan bought Carnegie Steel and combined it with other steel companies, the resulting U.S. Steel controlled 67% of U.S. steel production. It was capitalized at $1.4 billion — more than the total federal budget. No single private corporation had ever held this much productive power. It shocked even people accustomed to Gilded Age scale.

Q: What was the significance of Chicago's Union Stock Yards?
1: It was the site of the Haymarket Affair, where labor unrest in the meatpacking industry led to violence
2: It was the industrial complex that made Chicago the meatpacking capital of the world — a centralized facility where animals from across the Great Plains were processed by an assembly-line system that Upton Sinclair exposed in "The Jungle"
3: It was a public marketplace where small farmers sold livestock directly to consumers
4: It was a federal facility for regulating the quality of meat sold in interstate commerce
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Union Stock Yards was an industrial city within a city. Railroads delivered cattle; workers on specialized "disassembly lines" processed them in minutes; refrigerated rail cars shipped meat nationwide. Henry Ford later said he got the idea for the automobile assembly line from watching Chicago meatpacking. Sinclair's 1906 expose of conditions there triggered the Pure Food and Drug Act.

Q: What distinguished the "new immigration" of the 1880s-1910s from earlier immigration waves?
1: New immigrants were primarily from Western Europe — the same regions that had always sent immigrants
2: New immigrants came primarily from Southern and Eastern Europe (Italians, Poles, Russians, Greeks, Jews) rather than Western Europe — arriving in far larger numbers, speaking different languages, practicing different religions, and facing intense nativist hostility
3: New immigrants came primarily from Asia and Latin America rather than Europe
4: New immigrants were primarily skilled professionals rather than agricultural laborers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Earlier immigrants were mostly Protestant, English-speaking Northern Europeans who assimilated relatively easily. "New immigrants" from Italy, Poland, Russia, and the Austro-Hungarian Empire were Catholic, Orthodox, or Jewish; spoke unfamiliar languages; and settled in dense ethnic urban neighborhoods. Nativists argued they were unassimilable. In reality they followed the same path as earlier immigrants — just with more hostility along the way.

Q: What was the Social Gospel movement?
1: A Populist religious movement arguing God supported free silver coinage
2: A Protestant reform movement that argued Christianity required addressing poverty, inequality, and social injustice directly — not just saving individual souls — providing religious justification for Progressive Era reforms
3: A Catholic campaign to establish ethnic parishes and resist assimilation
4: A fundamentalist movement opposing the teaching of evolution in public schools
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Social Gospel leaders like Washington Gladden and Walter Rauschenbusch argued that sin was structural — you couldn't save souls while ignoring the slums they lived in. This provided moral fuel for Progressive reforms: settlement houses, labor laws, and anti-poverty programs. It reframed Christianity from otherworldly to this-worldly reform.

Q: What was the significance of Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)?
1: It struck down all segregation laws as violations of the 14th Amendment
2: The Supreme Court ruled that "separate but equal" facilities for Black and white Americans were constitutional, providing legal justification for Jim Crow segregation that lasted until Brown v. Board of Education in 1954
3: It ruled that literacy tests for voting were unconstitutional under the 15th Amendment
4: It declared the Chinese Exclusion Act unconstitutional as a violation of equal protection
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Homer Plessy (who was 7/8 white) challenged Louisiana's railroad segregation law. The Supreme Court ruled 7-1 that separate facilities were constitutional as long as they were "equal" — they never were. Plessy made segregation the law of the land for 58 years, giving legal backing to an entire system of racial oppression that included schools, hospitals, buses, and every aspect of public life.

Q: Who was W.E.B. Du Bois, and how did his approach differ from Booker T. Washington's?
1: Du Bois agreed with Washington but focused on rural rather than urban Black communities
2: Du Bois argued that Black Americans must demand immediate full political and civil equality, rejected Washington's accommodationism as surrendering rights, and co-founded the NAACP — representing a fundamentally different theory of how oppressed groups achieve liberation
3: Du Bois focused on economic self-sufficiency through cooperatives, while Washington focused on vocational education
4: Du Bois was a labor organizer who focused on integrating trade unions, while Washington focused on education
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Washington-Du Bois debate is one of the most important in American history. Washington said: accept segregation for now, build economic strength, earn equality later. Du Bois said: you cannot earn rights that are being stolen from you through accommodation — you must demand them. Du Bois' approach, through the NAACP and the civil rights movement, ultimately prevailed.

Q: What was the significance of Alfred Mahan's "The Influence of Sea Power upon History"?
1: It argued that the U.S. should build a large standing army to defend continental borders
2: Mahan argued that all great powers in history had depended on naval dominance, and that the U.S. must build a blue-water navy, acquire overseas bases, and dominate ocean trade routes to become a global power — directly shaping U.S. foreign policy in the 1890s
3: It was a critique of British imperialism warning the U.S. not to follow the same path
4: It proposed a theory of guerrilla warfare that the U.S. used against the Philippines
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Mahan gave American expansionists a strategic doctrine. His readers included Theodore Roosevelt, Henry Cabot Lodge, and Kaiser Wilhelm II. The U.S. began building a modern steel navy in the 1880s; by 1898 it was strong enough to defeat Spain's fleet in two battles in two oceans. Mahan's theory became American foreign policy reality.

Q: What was the Anti-Imperialist League, and who supported it?
1: A Populist organization that opposed the annexation of western territories
2: A coalition of prominent Americans — including Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie, and Samuel Gompers — who opposed U.S. acquisition of the Philippines and other overseas territories, arguing that colonial rule contradicted American democratic principles
3: A labor organization that opposed immigration from recently acquired territories
4: A congressional caucus that blocked ratification of the Treaty of Paris
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Anti-Imperialist League united strange bedfellows: Twain (moral outrage), Carnegie (it was bad business), Gompers (cheap colonial labor would undercut American workers), and intellectuals who argued you couldn't govern people without their consent — which was exactly what the Philippines demonstrated. They lost the political battle but were historically prescient.

Q: What was the significance of the Homestead Act in the context of westward expansion?
1: It successfully created a nation of independent small farmers across the Great Plains as intended
2: It was a genuine attempt to democratize land ownership but was undermined in practice by railroad land grants, land speculation, and the harsh reality of Plains farming — most homesteaders ultimately failed or sold to larger interests
3: It was a Populist reform that transferred railroad land back to individual farmers
4: It successfully resolved conflicts between settlers and Native Americans by creating clear legal boundaries
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Homestead Act had noble intentions — 160 acres to anyone who'd farm it for 5 years. But 160 acres was too small for viable Plains farming; railroads got far more land; speculators gamed the system; and droughts destroyed many claims. More land passed to railroads and corporations than to individual homesteaders. Good intentions, perverse outcomes.

Q: What was the significance of the 17th Amendment (1913) in the context of the Populist movement?
1: It gave women the right to vote, fulfilling a key Populist demand
2: It established the direct election of U.S. Senators — which had been explicitly demanded in the Populist Omaha Platform of 1892, showing that Populist ideas survived into the Progressive Era even after the party collapsed
3: It created a federal income tax, another Populist demand
4: It established Prohibition, the Populist temperance movement's greatest victory
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Populists demanded direct Senate elections because senators were then chosen by state legislatures that railroad money easily corrupted. The idea died with the Populist Party in 1896 but was revived by Progressives and passed in 1913. The 16th Amendment (income tax, also 1913) was another Populist idea. Their party died; their platform largely became law.

Q: What was the significance of the Granger movement?
1: It was the first attempt by Northern industrialists to organize workers into labor unions
2: It was an organization of farmers in the 1860s-70s that fought railroad price gouging through cooperative buying, lobbied for regulation, and achieved Granger Laws in several states — a direct precursor to Populism
3: It was a Native American cultural preservation movement in the Great Plains
4: It was a Progressive Era consumer protection organization focused on food safety
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Patrons of Husbandry (the Grange) was the first major farmer organization to fight back against railroad and grain elevator monopolies. They built cooperative stores, lobbied successfully for state railroad regulation (Granger Laws), and built the organizational infrastructure that the Farmers' Alliance and then the Populist Party inherited and expanded.

Q: What was the role of the Pinkerton National Detective Agency in Gilded Age labor conflicts?
1: A government agency that mediated between workers and employers to prevent strikes
2: A private security corporation hired by industrialists to spy on unions, infiltrate organizing drives, and provide armed strikebreakers — effectively functioning as a private army for corporate interests against labor
3: A labor organization that provided security for workers during strikes
4: A federal agency created by Congress to investigate labor unrest and recommend policy
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Pinkertons were capitalism's private police force. They infiltrated the Molly Maguires, spied on Knights of Labor chapters, and sent 300 armed men by barge to Homestead. At their peak they had more agents than the U.S. Army. The existence of private armies for hire by corporations — and the inability of workers to legally fight back — defined Gilded Age labor power imbalance.

Q: What was the significance of Standard Oil, and what strategy did Rockefeller use to build it?
1: Standard Oil was a steel company that used vertical integration to control steel production from ore to finished product
2: Rockefeller used horizontal integration — buying or destroying competing oil refineries until Standard Oil controlled over 90% of U.S. oil refining — along with secret railroad rebates that his competitors couldn't get, making true competition impossible
3: Standard Oil was the first corporation to use the assembly line, allowing it to outcompete rivals through efficiency alone
4: Rockefeller built Standard Oil through vertical integration, controlling oil wells, pipelines, and retail gas stations
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Rockefeller's genius was horizontal integration — eliminate competition at the refining stage. He negotiated secret railroad rebates that gave him lower shipping rates than competitors, then used the savings to undercut their prices, drive them to bankruptcy or buy them out. By 1882 Standard Oil controlled 91% of U.S. oil refining. The Sherman Act was passed largely with Standard Oil in mind.

Q: What was the significance of the 1900 census data showing that 60% of Northern city residents were immigrants or their children?
1: It proved that immigration policy had failed and immigrants were not assimilating
2: It demonstrated that American industrial cities were being fundamentally transformed by immigration — the urban working class was predominantly immigrant, making immigration central to any understanding of labor, politics, and culture in the Gilded Age
3: It led Congress to immediately pass the first major immigration restriction laws
4: It confirmed that native-born Americans were fleeing cities for the suburbs
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: By 1890, cities like New York, Chicago, and Boston were majority immigrant or children of immigrants. These were the workers in the factories, the voters in the Democratic machines, the families in the tenements. You cannot understand Gilded Age labor, politics, or urban culture without understanding immigration. The "American" working class was largely immigrant.

Q: What was the significance of Ida Tarbell's investigation of Standard Oil?
1: It directly led to Rockefeller's criminal conviction for monopolistic practices
2: Her 19-part series in McClure's Magazine (1902-04) documented Standard Oil's secret deals, corrupt tactics, and monopoly power through meticulous research — becoming the model for investigative journalism and directly contributing to the Supreme Court's 1911 breakup of Standard Oil
3: It exposed conditions in Chicago meatpacking plants and led to the Pure Food and Drug Act
4: It documented price-fixing by railroad companies, leading to the strengthening of the Interstate Commerce Commission
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Tarbell spent years going through financial records, court documents, and interviewing former Standard Oil employees. Her work was methodical, devastating, and personal — her father's oil company had been destroyed by Standard Oil's tactics. McClure's readership exploded. Roosevelt used it as political ammunition. The Supreme Court broke up Standard Oil in 1911 largely because public opinion, shaped by Tarbell, demanded it.

Q: What was the significance of the annexation of Hawaii?
1: Hawaii was annexed to protect its native Hawaiian population from exploitation by sugar planters
2: The annexation followed an illegal overthrow of Queen Liliuokalani by American sugar planters supported by U.S. Marines, and was justified primarily by Hawaii's strategic value as a Pacific naval base — illustrating how economic and military interests drove imperialism
3: Hawaii requested annexation voluntarily through a democratic referendum of its population
4: Hawaii was annexed in response to Japanese military threats to the islands
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Queen Liliuokalani was trying to reduce the political power of American sugar planters. They organized a coup with U.S. Marines "coincidentally" nearby. President Cleveland called it illegal and refused to annex. McKinley annexed it in 1898. Native Hawaiians, who were not consulted, became a minority in their own homeland. It is a textbook case of economic and strategic imperialism.

Q: What did the Farmers' Alliance try to do, and why did it ultimately lead to the Populist Party?
1: The Alliance was a labor union that organized farm workers for better wages, which evolved into a political party when employers refused to negotiate
2: The Alliance built cooperative economic institutions to help farmers escape railroad and bank exploitation; when those cooperatives were blocked by financial interests, farmers concluded political power was necessary — forming the Populist Party to seek legislative solutions
3: The Alliance was a Grange successor focused exclusively on lobbying for railroad regulation
4: The Alliance sought to maintain the gold standard against Eastern banking interests
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Alliance cooperatives tried to bypass exploitative middlemen by buying supplies collectively and marketing crops together. But banks refused to loan to cooperatives, railroads gave cooperatives the same terrible rates, and legal barriers blocked expansion. After cooperative economics failed, Populist politics was the logical next step. Economic organization led to political mobilization.

CHAPTER: Ch. 19 – American Empire: People & Places

Q: What was the significance of the USS Maine's sinking?
1: It was the event that conclusively proved Spanish aggression and justified the declaration of war
2: The mysterious explosion of an American battleship in Havana Harbor in 1898 — likely accidental but blamed on Spain by yellow journalists — became the emotional trigger that pushed Congress to declare war on Spain despite uncertain evidence of Spanish responsibility
3: It was a planned operation by the U.S. Navy to create a pretext for war with Spain
4: It was a Spanish torpedo attack that killed 500 American sailors, leaving no doubt about responsibility
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: "Remember the Maine, to hell with Spain!" was the rallying cry. Yellow journalists Hearst and Pulitzer blamed Spain immediately and with certainty. The cause was almost certainly an internal coal fire, not Spanish sabotage. But the emotional power of 260 American sailors killed was irresistible. The Maine shows how media can manufacture war fever from ambiguous facts.

Q: What was the significance of Commodore George Dewey's victory at Manila Bay?
1: It was a hard-fought naval battle that cost the U.S. heavy casualties but secured control of the Philippines
2: Dewey's fleet destroyed the entire Spanish Pacific fleet in a single morning without losing a single American sailor — demonstrating the overwhelming superiority of the modern U.S. Navy and immediately giving the U.S. control of the Philippines
3: It was the naval battle that destroyed the Spanish Atlantic fleet protecting Cuba
4: Dewey's victory allowed the U.S. to negotiate a ceasefire with Spain before any ground fighting occurred
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Battle of Manila Bay (May 1, 1898) lasted seven hours. Dewey's fleet destroyed 10 Spanish ships and killed 381 Spanish sailors. American casualties: zero. It was the most lopsided naval victory in modern warfare and instantly made Dewey a national hero. It also immediately raised the question: now that we have the Philippines, what do we do with them?

Q: What was the significance of Cuba in U.S. foreign policy during the late 19th century?
1: Cuba was primarily valued for its tobacco production, which American companies sought to control
2: Cuba was a Spanish colony 90 miles from Florida where American businesses had $50 million invested, where Spain was running brutal concentration camps to suppress a rebellion, and where yellow journalists found perfect material to push the U.S. toward war
3: Cuba was primarily a naval base that the U.S. sought to acquire to project power in the Caribbean
4: Cuba's importance was purely strategic — no significant American economic interests existed there before the war
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Cuba had everything needed for a war: American money at risk, a sympathetic rebel population, Spanish atrocities (some real, some exaggerated), a media hungry for drama, and politicians looking for a cause. After the war, the Platt Amendment gave the U.S. the right to intervene in Cuba's affairs, making it an independent country in name only.

Q: What was the Platt Amendment?
1: A U.S. law giving the Philippines limited self-governance under American oversight
2: A condition attached to Cuba's constitution that gave the U.S. the permanent right to intervene in Cuban affairs, maintain a naval base at Guantánamo Bay, and approve Cuban treaties — making Cuba nominally independent but effectively a U.S. protectorate
3: An amendment to the Monroe Doctrine limiting European intervention in the Caribbean
4: A congressional resolution authorizing the Spanish-American War
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Cuban independence was real in name only. The Platt Amendment, forced on Cuba under threat of continued U.S. occupation, gave the U.S. almost unlimited right to intervene. The U.S. did intervene militarily in Cuba multiple times. The naval base at Guantánamo Bay, still in U.S. hands today, is a direct legacy of the Platt Amendment.

Q: Which of the following BEST explains why the U.S. chose to keep rather than free the Philippines after the Spanish-American War?
1: Filipinos requested American annexation because they feared reconquest by Spain
2: A combination of strategic (Pacific naval base), economic (Asian trade access), and ideological (Social Darwinist duty to "civilize") motivations convinced McKinley that the U.S. should keep the Philippines despite the contradiction with American anti-colonial values
3: Congress voted overwhelmingly to keep the Philippines against McKinley's wishes
4: The Philippines was kept as a temporary measure until the Philippines could demonstrate self-governance
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: McKinley famously said he got down on his knees and prayed, then concluded God told him the U.S. must "uplift and civilize and Christianize" the Filipinos — ignoring that most were already Catholic. The real reasons: Manila Bay was perfect for trade with Asia; competitors Britain and Germany would grab it otherwise; Social Darwinism said weaker peoples needed stronger nations' guidance. McKinley dressed power in moral clothing.

Q: Which of the following BEST describes the overall pattern of U.S. policy toward non-white groups in the late 19th century?
1: The U.S. was unique among industrial nations in its commitment to racial equality during this period
2: A consistent pattern of racial hierarchy — Native American dispossession, Black disenfranchisement, Chinese exclusion, and Philippine colonization — was embedded in federal policy across all regions and administrations, revealing white supremacy as a structural feature of American governance, not just a Southern regional attitude
3: Racial injustice was primarily a Southern phenomenon while Northern states maintained equality
4: U.S. racial policy was gradually improving throughout the period, with steady progress toward equality
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Look at the full picture: the federal government dispossessed Native Americans (Dawes Act), allowed Black disenfranchisement (end of Reconstruction), excluded Chinese immigrants (Chinese Exclusion Act), and colonized Filipinos (Philippine-American War). Different groups, different mechanisms, same underlying logic of white supremacist hierarchy. This is not regional — it is national policy.

Q: Which of the following BEST explains why the Gilded Age is called the "Gilded Age"?
1: It was an era of great prosperity and equality that literally made America golden
2: Mark Twain coined the term to suggest the era had a shiny surface appearance of prosperity and progress that concealed a corrupt, unequal, and unjust core — enormous wealth for a few built on exploitation, corruption, and suffering for many
3: It refers to the gold standard that dominated economic policy debate throughout the period
4: It refers to the "golden age" of immigration when the U.S. welcomed all newcomers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Twain and Charles Dudley Warner wrote "The Gilded Age: A Tale of Today" in 1873, satirizing an era of spectacular wealth, political corruption, and social inequality. "Gilded" not "Golden" — gold on the outside, something else underneath. Carnegie's libraries, Rockefeller's philanthropy, and robber baron mansions were the glittering surface. The Homestead Strike, Haymarket, and lynching were what was underneath.


Q: Which of the following BEST explains why Gilded Age industrialization simultaneously increased national wealth and worker poverty?
1: Workers were unskilled and therefore deserved low wages under a fair market
2: The benefits of industrial productivity went overwhelmingly to capital owners through wage suppression, union busting, and monopoly pricing — workers lacked political and legal power to claim a fair share of what they produced
3: The U.S. economy was simply too small to distribute wealth broadly while also industrializing
4: Immigrant workers voluntarily accepted low wages, driving down standards for all workers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: This is the core paradox of the Gilded Age. GDP grew massively. Workers produced unprecedented output. But the rules of the game — no unions, no minimum wage, no labor protections, government on the side of employers — meant productivity gains flowed to owners. The question of who gets the gains from economic growth is fundamentally political, not just economic.

Q: The phrase "Remember the Maine, to hell with Spain!" best illustrates which phenomenon?
1: The power of government propaganda to manufacture support for foreign policy
2: How yellow journalism could transform an ambiguous event into a war cry — showing that emotional, sensationalized media coverage can override careful analysis of facts and drive public opinion toward war
3: The genuine anger of the American public at Spanish colonial brutality in Cuba
4: The effectiveness of anti-war movements in the 1890s
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Maine probably sank from an internal coal fire, not a Spanish bomb. But Hearst and Pulitzer's papers screamed "Spanish treachery!" with banner headlines and dramatic illustrations. By the time investigations began, public outrage had already built an irresistible momentum toward war. It is a case study in how media frames events and manufactures emotional consensus.

Q: What did Frederick Jackson Turner mean when he said the frontier gave Americans their "democratic character"?
1: That the frontier provided land for independent farming that created economic equality and therefore political equality among white American men
2: That the experience of constantly rebuilding civilization from scratch on the frontier — without inherited aristocracy or rigid class structures — produced Americans who were individualistic, self-reliant, and democratic in spirit, unlike class-bound Europeans
3: That the military conquest of the frontier taught Americans to respect democratic institutions
4: That Native American democratic traditions were absorbed into American culture through frontier contact
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Turner argued that having cheap land as a "safety valve" meant American workers didn't become a permanent proletariat — they could move West and start fresh. This prevented the class consciousness of European workers and created American individualism. Historians have criticized this for erasing Native peoples, women, and minorities from the story, and for being economically inaccurate about how much the frontier actually functioned as a safety valve.

Q: What was the effect of the Spanish-American War on the United States' global position?
1: The war's high cost weakened U.S. economic standing relative to European powers
2: The U.S. emerged from the 10-week war as a recognized global imperial power with territories in the Caribbean and Pacific, a powerful modern navy, and international prestige — completing its transformation from regional to world power
3: The war improved U.S.-European relations by demonstrating that America would cooperate with European powers in global affairs
4: The war primarily demonstrated American military weakness, as most battles were won by Cuban and Filipino allies rather than U.S. forces
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Before 1898 the U.S. was a major economic but minor military-imperial power. After 1898 it had Cuba as a protectorate, Puerto Rico and Guam as territories, the Philippines as a colony, and a navy that had defeated two Spanish fleets. Europe noticed. The 20th century would be shaped by this emergence. The Spanish-American War was America's coming-out party as a global power.

Q: The Farmers' Alliance, Knights of Labor, and Populist Party all failed to permanently change the political economy of the United States in the Gilded Age. Which of the following BEST explains why?
1: Their leaders were personally corrupt and mismanaged their organizations
2: They faced a combination of overwhelming economic power from industrialists and railroads, hostile government intervention, racial divisions that prevented cross-race solidarity, and a political system (money in elections, electoral college) structurally biased against agrarian reformers
3: Their policy proposals were poorly thought out and would not have actually helped their members
4: Most workers and farmers were too satisfied with their conditions to sustain reform movements
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Reform failed not because reformers were weak or wrong, but because the system they faced had overwhelming advantages: unlimited money for campaigns, government troops for strikes, courts for injunctions, media for propaganda, and racial divisions that kept poor whites and poor Blacks from uniting. McKinley spent $7 million in 1896; Bryan spent $300,000. The deck was stacked.

Q: Jane Addams and Hull House represent which approach to Gilded Age poverty?
1: Radical socialism — organizing the poor to overthrow the capitalist system
2: Settlement house reformism — educated, middle-class reformers living in poor communities to provide direct services (English classes, childcare, legal aid) while advocating for structural reforms like labor laws, building knowledge of poverty from direct experience
3: Philanthropy — wealthy donors funding institutions to help the poor without living among them
4: Political machine organizing — exchanging social services for political loyalty and votes
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hull House (1889) was revolutionary in approach: Addams didn't just donate money — she moved into a Chicago immigrant neighborhood and built a community center offering classes, a kindergarten, a gym, and legal help. From this direct experience she lobbied for labor laws, child labor restrictions, and public health. Settlement houses were the laboratory of Progressive Era reform.

Q: Which of the following BEST describes the economic situation of Great Plains farmers in the 1880s-90s?
1: Farmers were generally prosperous but politically frustrated by lack of representation
2: Farmers were caught in a devastating squeeze — falling crop prices as technology increased supply globally, high railroad rates to ship crops to market, tight credit from banks that could foreclose on bad harvests, and rising costs for manufactured goods protected by tariffs — making farming increasingly economically impossible
3: Farmers were primarily hurt by competition from Southern agriculture that had been rebuilt after Reconstruction
4: Farmers' main problem was lack of land — not enough homesteaders had been able to claim land under the Homestead Act
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Plains farmer's situation was a perfect economic trap. Global wheat and corn prices fell as production worldwide increased. Railroad rates were set by monopolies with no competition. Bank loans came due regardless of weather or prices. Manufactured goods were expensive because of protective tariffs. Farmers worked harder and earned less every year. Populism was a rational response to an irrational economic situation.

Q: What does the term "vertical integration" mean, and which industrialist best exemplified it?
1: The strategy of buying competing companies in the same industry; best exemplified by Rockefeller's Standard Oil
2: The strategy of controlling every stage of production from raw materials to finished product to distribution; best exemplified by Carnegie's steel empire which controlled iron ore mines, coal fields, railroads, and steel mills
3: The strategy of merging with foreign companies to access global markets; used by J.P. Morgan to create U.S. Steel
4: A government regulatory approach that required corporations to report financial information vertically through regulatory agencies
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Carnegie controlled everything: iron ore mines in Minnesota, coal fields in Pennsylvania, ships on the Great Lakes, railroads to Pittsburgh, steel mills, and distribution. By owning every input, he eliminated supplier profit and could undercut any competitor. Rockefeller used horizontal integration (buying competitors at the same stage); Carnegie used vertical (owning every stage). Both created monopolistic power through different routes.
