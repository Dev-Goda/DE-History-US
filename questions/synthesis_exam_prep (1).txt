CHAPTER: Synthesis – Exam Prep

Q: The Haymarket Affair (1886) and the Pullman Strike (1894) both resulted in:
1: Major legislative victories for the labor movement
2: The government siding with workers against corporate power
3: Federal intervention that damaged organized labor's public image and power
4: Formation of new more powerful labor unions immediately after
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: In both cases the federal government intervened on the side of corporations — troops to break strikes, courts to jail leaders. Both events set back labor organizing and proved that the state would not be neutral in labor-capital conflicts during the Gilded Age.

Q: Social Darwinism and the Gospel of Wealth both served what function for wealthy industrialists?
1: They justified economic inequality and discouraged government regulation
2: They promoted charity as a way to redistribute wealth equally
3: They supported labor unions as a natural outcome of competition
4: They encouraged government to break up monopolies
ANSWER: 1
EXPLAIN: Social Darwinism said inequality was natural and inevitable (don't regulate). The Gospel of Wealth said the wealthy would fix inequality through philanthropy (no need to regulate). Both conclusions conveniently landed on "don't regulate business" — serving industrialists' interests perfectly.

Q: The Populist Omaha Platform and the Knights of Labor both reflected:
1: Urban skilled workers wanting to maintain wages
2: Frustration with concentrated economic power and demands for government reform
3: Support for the gold standard as economic stability
4: Opposition to immigration as a threat to American workers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Whether farmers facing railroad monopolies or factory workers facing industrial barons, both groups identified the same core problem: economic power was dangerously concentrated and the government was not protecting working people. Both called for systemic reform.

Q: The Dawes Act and Chinese Exclusion Act both reflected:
1: Efforts to help minority groups integrate into American economic life
2: Federal policies that undermined or restricted non-white groups' rights
3: Responses to labor union demands to protect American workers
4: Both were declared unconstitutional shortly after passage
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The same racial hierarchies that justified dispossessing Native Americans of communal land (Dawes Act) and banning Chinese immigration by race (Exclusion Act) also justified Jim Crow laws and lynching. These were expressions of a consistent white supremacist political culture, not isolated incidents.

Q: Turner's Frontier Thesis is historically problematic because:
1: It overstated how much the frontier shaped Southern culture
2: It ignored the economic costs of westward expansion
3: It erased Native Americans, women, and minorities from the story of western development
4: Historians disproved it within ten years
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Turner's "frontier" was only "free" because Native peoples who lived there were violently displaced. His story of heroic individualism had no room for the Chinese who built railroads, Mexican vaqueros who taught cowboys, or women who homesteaded alongside men.

Q: Sand Creek, the Long Walk, and the Dawes Act all represent:
1: Native American military victories
2: A consistent U.S. policy using violence and legislation to displace and destroy Native peoples
3: Individual state actions the federal government later condemned
4: Failed experiments in Native American self-governance
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: These events span 1864-1887 and use different tactics — massacre, forced relocation, legal dispossession — but represent the same goal: removing Native peoples from their land through whatever means necessary to open it to white settlement.

Q: The Open Door Policy in China and the Roosevelt Corollary in Latin America both reflect:
1: Spreading democratic elections to foreign nations
2: Asserting U.S. economic and political power abroad without formal colonization
3: Building military alliances to counterbalance British imperial power
4: Isolating the U.S. from foreign conflicts
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Neither policy involved formally claiming territory. Instead both asserted U.S. dominance through economic rights (Open Door) or military intervention rights (Roosevelt Corollary). This was "informal empire" — the economic benefits of imperialism without the administrative costs.

Q: The Philippine-American War directly contradicted which American value used to justify the Spanish-American War?
1: Free trade and open markets
2: Self-determination and opposition to colonial rule
3: Freedom of the seas
4: The right to bear arms against tyranny
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The U.S. entered the Spanish-American War partly claiming it was fighting for Cuba's right to self-determination. Then it denied the Philippines the same right. Anti-imperialists like Mark Twain argued this hypocrisy revealed the real motivation was power and commerce, not freedom.

Q: The Great Railroad Strike (1877) and Pullman Strike (1894) were similar in cause because:
1: Both were caused by government attempts to nationalize railroads
2: Both were triggered by wage cuts during economic downturns
3: Both were organized by the Knights of Labor as national strategy
4: Both began as peaceful protests that workers turned violent
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: In both cases, corporations cut wages during economic downturns to protect profits while expecting workers to absorb the pain. This pattern — workers bearing the cost of downturns while ownership protected its returns — was the central grievance of Gilded Age labor relations.

Q: Ida B. Wells, Ida Tarbell, and Jane Addams can all be described as:
1: Leaders of the women's suffrage movement focused on voting rights
2: Reformers who used investigative journalism or community organizing to expose injustice
3: Politicians who ran for office on Progressive Party platforms
4: Business leaders who became philanthropists after making fortunes in industry
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: All three used the tools available to them — journalism (Wells, Tarbell) and community organizing (Addams) — to expose injustice and build pressure for change. None held elected office. All three demonstrated that women could be powerful political actors outside formal politics.

Q: Settlement houses and the Farmers Alliance both addressed economic hardship by:
1: Calling for government ownership of all production
2: Creating community-based cooperative organizations to help members survive economic pressure
3: Lobbying Congress for protective tariffs
4: Encouraging members to move to cities for industrial jobs
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Both built from the bottom up — the Alliance with cooperative buying and selling to cut out exploitative middlemen, settlement houses with cooperative social services in immigrant neighborhoods. Both believed in collective community solutions rather than waiting for government action.

Q: The Homestead Act and Transcontinental Railroad were both designed to:
1: Protect Native American rights by creating clear settlement boundaries
2: Encourage westward settlement and develop the American economy
3: Primarily benefit Southern farmers recovering from the Civil War
4: Create government jobs to address unemployment in Eastern cities
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Both were part of the Republican "free labor" vision of the West — a place for ordinary Americans to build independent economic lives. In practice, both also accelerated Native American dispossession and enriched corporations far more than individual settlers.

Q: Why is the 1896 Bryan vs McKinley election considered a turning point?
1: It was the last election where a third party received electoral votes
2: McKinley's victory signaled the defeat of agrarian Populism and the triumph of industrial capitalism
3: Bryan's defeat led directly to the Sherman Antitrust Act
4: It was the first election where women could vote in multiple states
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: 1896 was the last serious challenge from agrarian reform to industrial capitalism's political dominance. After McKinley's decisive win, the Populist Party collapsed. Future reform would come through the Progressive movement — urban, middle-class, and accepting of capitalism while seeking to regulate it.

Q: Which comparison of Knights of Labor vs AFL is most accurate?
1: Both used radical socialist tactics and the AFL was just better funded
2: The Knights pursued broad social reform with inclusive membership; the AFL focused narrowly on skilled workers' wages through collective bargaining
3: The AFL was more radical; the Knights were conservative
4: Both rejected strikes and differed only in membership requirements
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Remember: Knights = broad, inclusive, reform-minded, declined after Haymarket. AFL = narrow, skilled workers only, practical "bread and butter" approach, survived long-term. The AFL's pragmatism was less inspiring but far more durable.

Q: Reconstruction's end and the rise of Jim Crow illustrate which historical pattern?
1: Southern states willingly integrated but were blocked by Northern politicians
2: When federal enforcement of Black civil rights ended, white supremacist violence and law filled the void
3: Black Americans voluntarily moved North allowing the South to revert to pre-war conditions
4: The Supreme Court consistently protected Black voting rights but states found workarounds
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments created legal equality. But when federal troops withdrew from the South (1877 Compromise), there was no enforcement. White supremacists used violence, law, and economic coercion to systematically reverse Black political gains — showing rights without enforcement are hollow.

Q: The Great Merger Movement and the Great Railroad Strike both illustrate:
1: The failure of capitalism in the Gilded Age
2: The tension between concentrated corporate power and the needs of workers and consumers
3: The government's growing willingness to regulate big business
4: How immigration fueled both industrial growth and labor unrest
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Merger Movement concentrated economic power in giant corporations. The railroad strikes showed what happened to workers trapped inside that system. Both are different sides of the same story: industrial capitalism generating enormous wealth and enormous suffering simultaneously.

Q: Yellow Journalism and Wild West shows both demonstrate that in the late 1800s:
1: Americans had little interest in events outside their communities
2: Mass media and entertainment were powerful tools for shaping public opinion and national identity
3: The government used media to promote imperialist policies
4: Immigrant communities were excluded from mainstream American culture
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Yellow journalism manipulated public opinion toward war; Wild West shows manufactured a national myth about western expansion. Both show that the Gilded Age was the beginning of modern mass media's power to shape what Americans believed was true about their history and identity.

Q: Which Progressive Era reform came directly from the Populist platform?
1: Women's suffrage (19th Amendment)
2: Prohibition (18th Amendment)
3: Direct election of U.S. Senators (17th Amendment, 1913)
4: Abolition of the Electoral College
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The Omaha Platform (1892) explicitly called for direct election of Senators (then chosen by state legislatures, often corrupted by railroad money). The 17th Amendment finally enacted this in 1913 — one of many Populist ideas that survived into the Progressive Era.

Q: Mahan's naval theory, the Roosevelt Corollary, and the acquisition of Hawaii and the Philippines all point to:
1: An isolationist U.S. foreign policy focused on continental defense
2: A deliberate late 19th-century turn toward U.S. imperialism driven by economic and military interests
3: A response to European aggression forcing the U.S. to expand defensively
4: Congressional pressure on the executive branch to acquire overseas territories
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: These weren't random or reactive — they were a coherent program. Mahan provided the theory (naval power = greatness), Hawaii and the Philippines provided the bases, and the Roosevelt Corollary provided regional dominance. Together they mark a deliberate decision to make the U.S. a global imperial power.

Q: How did urbanization change American politics in the late 1800s?
1: It strengthened the Republican Party in the South
2: It created powerful political machines, immigrant voting blocs, and new demands for progressive reform
3: It reduced political participation as factory workers had no time to vote
4: It led to decline of the Democratic Party
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Cities became the Gilded Age political battleground — machines like Tammany Hall organized immigrant voters into Democratic blocs, while Progressive reformers tried to break machine power. Modern urban politics — with its tensions between machine organization and reform movements — was born in this era.

Q: Why were industrialists like Carnegie and Rockefeller both praised AND called Robber Barons?
1: They were praised for military service and criticized for business tactics
2: They were praised for creating jobs and charity, but criticized for exploiting workers and crushing competition through unethical practices
3: They were praised by workers and only criticized by rival businessmen
4: They were praised for the gold standard and criticized for opposing immigration
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Carnegie's libraries and Rockefeller's philanthropy were real and valuable. But they were built on destroyed competitors, bribed politicians, Pinkertons shooting strikers, and workers earning poverty wages. The "Robber Baron" critique said philanthropy was no substitute for justice in the first place.
