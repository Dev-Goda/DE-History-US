CHAPTER: Ch. 18 – Industrial America (Deep)

Q: "Gateway Cities" were significant because:
1: They were the only cities allowed to house immigrant workers under federal law
2: They served as entry points for millions of immigrants, becoming centers of ethnic neighborhoods and industrial labor
3: They were the wealthiest cities offering the most factory jobs
4: They were government-designed centers for processing immigrants
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Cities like New York (Ellis Island), Boston, and Chicago were first destinations for millions of immigrants. They became ethnic neighborhoods, political machine strongholds, and industrial labor hubs — fundamentally reshaping American urban culture.

Q: The Second Industrial Revolution (1870-1914) was driven by:
1: Horse-drawn transportation, canals, and cotton
2: Steel production, electricity, oil, and mass production
3: Farming machinery, crop rotation, and irrigation
4: Textile mills, coal mining, and steam power only
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Second Industrial Revolution moved beyond steam-and-textile to steel (Carnegie), oil (Rockefeller), electricity (Edison), and mass production — transforming the U.S. into a global economic superpower in just a few decades.

Q: "Push factors" in immigration are:
1: Policies welcoming immigrants with job offers
2: Reasons attracting immigrants to a new country
3: Reasons compelling people to leave their home countries, like poverty or war
4: Government programs relocating immigrants within the U.S.
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Push factors drive people OUT of their homeland (poverty, famine, persecution, war). Pull factors draw people TO a new country (jobs, land, freedom). The massive New Immigration wave was driven by both European hardship and American economic opportunity.

Q: "Chain migration" refers to:
1: Forced migration of enslaved people across the Atlantic
2: Immigrants following earlier migrants from their homeland to settle in the same area
3: Immigrants forming long chains of workers in factory assembly lines
4: The legal process of sponsoring a relative's immigration application
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Chain migration built America's ethnic neighborhoods — one immigrant from a specific village settled in Boston, then wrote home, and cousins followed, creating a community. This preserved culture and provided support networks but also created enclaves that sometimes slowed assimilation.

Q: George Washington Plunkitt of Tammany Hall exemplified:
1: The progressive reform movement cleaning up city politics
2: The culture of urban political machines that dominated city governance
3: The Socialist Party's platform on campaign finance
4: How immigrant communities resisted corrupt political bosses
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Plunkitt distinguished "honest graft" (using political knowledge for personal profit without stealing) from outright corruption. His frank admissions revealed how Tammany Hall operated — providing real services to constituents in exchange for reliable votes and political loyalty.

Q: Immigrants maintained culture through all of the following EXCEPT:
1: Mutual-aid societies
2: Parish churches
3: Workmen's clubs
4: Rotary clubs
ANSWER: 4
EXPLAIN: Rotary clubs were mainstream American civic organizations for business professionals — not immigrant cultural institutions. Mutual-aid societies, ethnic parish churches, and workmen's clubs were the building blocks of immigrant community life in American cities.

Q: By 1890, about what percentage of northern city residents were immigrants or their children?
1: 20%
2: 40%
3: 60%
4: 80%
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: About 60% of northern city residents were either immigrants or their American-born children — showing how profoundly "New Immigration" from Southern and Eastern Europe was reshaping cities like New York, Chicago, and Boston.

Q: Immigrants in late 19th-century cities predominantly voted:
1: Republican
2: Democratic
3: Populist
4: Progressive
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Urban political machines like Tammany Hall (Democratic) provided immigrants with jobs, housing help, legal aid, and social services in exchange for reliable votes. The Republican Party was seen as the party of Protestant Anglo-American elites.

Q: Thomas Edison's most transformative contribution was:
1: Inventing the steam engine and making coal power efficient
2: Developing practical electrical power systems including the light bulb and phonograph
3: Designing the first automated assembly line
4: Creating the telegraph
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Edison developed the incandescent light bulb (1879) and then the entire infrastructure to deliver electricity to homes and businesses — power plants, wiring, meters. He industrialized invention itself, creating the modern research laboratory model.

Q: The phonograph was historically significant because:
1: It let workers communicate instantly across factories
2: It helped create the mass entertainment and music industry
3: It replaced the telegraph as main long-distance communication
4: It recorded political speeches for the first time
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Edison's phonograph (1877) let people experience music without a live performance for the first time in history. This eventually created the recording industry — fundamentally changing how humans experienced art and entertainment.

Q: Rockefeller built Standard Oil into a monopoly through:
1: Vertical integration controlling wells through pipelines
2: Horizontal integration absorbing competing refineries
3: Government subsidies giving exclusive drilling rights
4: Patent control blocking competitors from refining oil
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Horizontal integration means dominating one stage of production by absorbing all competitors. Rockefeller combined this with secret railroad rebates that undercut rivals' costs — eventually controlling 90% of U.S. oil refining by the 1880s.

Q: Carnegie's "Gospel of Wealth" argued:
1: Wealth should be taxed heavily to fund government
2: The wealthy have a moral obligation to distribute their wealth for the public good
3: Poverty is divine punishment requiring no charity
4: Business profits should be fully reinvested
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Carnegie argued the wealthy were society's best stewards of capital — accumulate aggressively but then give it away wisely for public benefit. His philanthropy was real but didn't require changing the system creating the inequality in the first place.

Q: The "Tainted Money Debate" concerned:
1: Politicians accepting money from foreign governments
2: Whether churches should accept donations from John D. Rockefeller
3: Whether labor unions could legally fund campaigns
4: Whether the government should tax inherited wealth
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: When Rockefeller offered millions to churches and universities, critics asked whether accepting money earned through exploiting workers and crushing competition made those institutions morally complicit — a pioneering debate about ethical fundraising.

Q: "Muscular Christianity" linked:
1: Missionary work abroad to U.S. imperialism
2: Physical strength and athletic bodies to moral virtue and Christian values
3: Military service to spiritual salvation
4: Church attendance to political activism
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Muscular Christianity argued a vigorous body reflected a vigorous Christian soul. It drove the founding of the YMCA, organized collegiate sports, and the cult of physical fitness — partly a response to anxieties that industrialization was making men "soft."

Q: The "Lost Cause" ideology portrayed the Confederacy as:
1: A necessary evil that was ultimately wrong about slavery
2: A noble cause defending Southern culture and states' rights, not slavery
3: A failed democratic experiment the South regretted
4: A humanitarian effort to preserve an agricultural way of life
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: The Lost Cause myth reframed the Civil War as noble defense of Southern culture against Northern aggression, deliberately minimizing slavery's role. It helped justify continued racial hierarchy in the Jim Crow South and influenced textbooks for decades.

Q: "New South" boosters wanted:
1: A return to plantation agriculture
2: Industrial development while maintaining white supremacy
3: Full racial integration as a foundation for economic growth
4: Federal reparations to rebuild Southern infrastructure
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Henry Grady and other boosters wanted Northern investment and industrial modernization, but explicitly within a white supremacist framework. They wanted the economic benefits of industrial development without Reconstruction's racial equality goals.

Q: From 1880 to 1950, approximately how many African Americans were lynched?
1: Around 500
2: Around 1,500
3: Around 5,000
4: Around 10,000
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Approximately 5,000 African Americans were lynched in this period. These were often public spectacles of racial terror, sometimes advertised in newspapers, used to maintain white supremacy and prevent Black economic and political advancement.

Q: Ida B. Wells is historically important because:
1: She led the women's suffrage movement in the South
2: She founded the NAACP as its first president
3: She led an anti-lynching campaign exposing racial terror
4: She wrote "The Yellow Wallpaper"
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Wells investigated dozens of lynchings, demolishing the justification that they were responses to Black male criminality. She showed they were tools of racial and economic control — used when Black men became "too successful" or politically active. She was forced to flee the South after her printing press was destroyed.

Q: Jim Crow laws received legal backing from:
1: Marbury v. Madison (1803)
2: Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)
3: Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) upholding "separate but equal"
4: McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) ruled "separate but equal" facilities were constitutional — giving legal cover to decades of mandated segregation in schools, transportation, restaurants, and public spaces. It wasn't reversed until Brown v. Board of Education in 1954.

Q: Black disenfranchisement was enforced through all of the following EXCEPT:
1: Poll taxes
2: Literacy tests
3: The Dawes Act
4: Violence and intimidation
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The Dawes Act dealt with Native American land allotment — nothing to do with Black voting rights. Poll taxes, literacy tests (administered unfairly), grandfather clauses, white-only primaries, and outright violence all worked together to strip Black Americans of the voting rights guaranteed by the 15th Amendment.

Q: Jane Addams founded Hull House primarily to:
1: Train skilled workers for industrial jobs
2: Serve immigrants and the urban poor through education and community services
3: Lobby Congress for women's suffrage
4: Offer a retreat for wealthy women interested in social reform
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Hull House (1889) was a settlement house where middle-class reformers lived in immigrant neighborhoods providing direct services: English classes, childcare, job training, legal aid, and cultural programs. Addams believed you had to live alongside poverty to understand and address it.

Q: Settlement houses were important to the Progressive Era because:
1: They trained factory managers in Taylorist methods
2: They were community centers providing education and services to help the urban poor
3: They were government-funded housing projects
4: They were headquarters for labor union activities
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Settlement house residents documented working conditions, infant mortality, and sweatshop labor — gathering the evidence that built the case for Progressive reform legislation. They combined direct service with political advocacy in a new reform model.

Q: "Scientific Motherhood" emphasized:
1: Women working in science labs instead of the home
2: Child-rearing guided by expert advice and scientific principles
3: Government control over family size
4: Teaching children science in public schools
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Progressive Era "scientific motherhood" told middle-class women to follow pediatric and nutritional expert advice rather than folk wisdom. It professionalized parenting but also increased pressure on mothers and positioned male experts as authorities over women's domestic lives.

Q: "The Yellow Wallpaper" (1892) is considered early feminist literature because:
1: It advocated for women's right to vote
2: It exposed unequal pay between men and women
3: It critiqued the treatment of women's mental health and confinement in a patriarchal society
4: It celebrated women's growing role in industrial production
ANSWER: 3
EXPLAIN: The narrator is confined to a room by her physician husband for "nervous exhaustion" — a common diagnosis used to control women. As she fixates on the wallpaper, the story becomes a metaphor for how patriarchal society imprisons women's minds and imaginations.

Q: Vaudeville was significant to American culture because:
1: It was entertainment exclusively for immigrant audiences
2: It shaped popular culture as a major form of diverse urban entertainment
3: It was performed exclusively for wealthy audiences
4: It was a government-sponsored program for immigrant performers
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Vaudeville was America's first mass entertainment form — traveling shows featuring comedy, music, acrobatics, and novelty acts played to diverse urban audiences regardless of class or ethnicity. It launched careers of future film and radio stars and created the template for modern variety entertainment.

Q: Charlie Chaplin's "Tramp" character resonated because:
1: He represented the heroic self-made businessman of the Gilded Age
2: He symbolized the struggles of the common man during the industrial age
3: He was a comic representation of wealthy industrialists
4: He mocked immigrants for comic effect
ANSWER: 2
EXPLAIN: Chaplin's Tramp — always poor, always dignified, always in conflict with police and authority — resonated globally as a symbol of the ordinary person navigating an industrial world designed for the wealthy. It was comedy with genuine social critique built in.
